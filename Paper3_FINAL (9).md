## Abstract

This paper addresses a problem in the inference of non-stationary multivariate time series on Riemannian manifolds: given a *single* multichannel record that crosses an abrupt transition, how to decide whether the post-transition dynamics is a directed (drift-dominated) reorganization, an undirected (diffusion-dominated) random walk, or a collapse of structure — from one realization, without an ensemble. The standard discriminators for this question are ensemble laws (the displacement-scaling contrast $\mathbb{E}[d_R]\propto t$ versus $\sqrt{t}$) that are not identifiable from a single trajectory, which is exactly the regime that abrupt transitions in expensive-to-observe systems impose: one record per transition. The contribution is a single-trajectory ($N{=}1$) demarcation protocol for state-transition dynamics represented on a curved state space, together with its estimability conditions and its declared failure modes.

The construction has three parts. First, the instantaneous state of a multichannel system is represented as a point in a Riemannian vector bundle: the base is a product of two trace-normalized symmetric-positive-definite (SPD) covariance-shape manifolds — one estimated on a slow (macro) timescale, one on a fast (micro) timescale of the same signal — and the fibre carries a normalized cross-scale coupling statistic. Trace normalization removes global-power and broadband-gain confounds, so that what is measured is correlation geometry rather than amplitude. Second, the tangent-space trajectory is modeled by a stochastic differential equation with a Poisson–Lévy jump term added to drift and Gaussian diffusion; the jump intensity is tied to the local conditional residual variance of the estimator, so an abrupt transition is represented as a discrete jump localized at the time of maximal predictive breakdown rather than as a smooth slide requiring unbounded drift. Third, a non-stationarity protocol — asymmetric causal windowing with a conditional reset of the estimator's autocorrelation, an effective-degrees-of-freedom correction, and tangent-space shrinkage with a strict eigenvalue floor — makes the geometry estimable on the short, non-stationary records abrupt transitions produce.

The single-trajectory demarcation replaces the ensemble displacement-scaling law with a path-wise likelihood-ratio test: the trajectory is anti-developed once to a fixed tangent space by Cartan (Eells–Elworthy–Malliavin) development so that Girsanov's theorem identifies the drift from a single path with curvature entering only as second-order holonomy; a covariate-anchored generalized likelihood ratio with a bipower (jump-robust) diffusion scale separates a genuine jump from a diffusive spike; and a $T_{\text{eff}}$-calibrated reference makes the test lose power gracefully rather than hallucinate structure when records are short. A declared precondition, established by simulation, is that the jump must be anchored to an estimator-derived predictability covariate (the local conditional residual variance): blind jump search under heavy-tailed diffusion produces false positives and is proscribed. Demarcation among the three regimes — asymptotic geodesic drift, isotropic fibre dispersion, structural rank collapse — is anchored to asymptotic stability under a Lyapunov criterion and to the long-term stationarity of Crutchfield–Young statistical complexity, on a timescale long enough to exclude transient confounds. The framework is presented with the carrier/envelope phase–amplitude coupling of neurophysiological state transitions as its worked instance, but the construction is stated abstractly enough to apply to any system with separable slow/fast scales and a trackable cross-scale coupling; no patient data are analyzed, and the protocol is offered as an executable, rejectable method, not an empirical result.

**Keywords:** Riemannian geometry; SPD manifolds; vector bundles; Sasaki metric; jump-diffusion; Poisson–Lévy process; stochastic development; Girsanov likelihood ratio; bipower variation; cross-scale coupling; statistical complexity; Lyapunov stability; single-trajectory inference; non-stationary time series; state-transition demarcation.

**Scope and non-claims.** Four delimitations hold throughout. First, this is a *methods and protocol* paper, not an empirical-results paper: it presents no measured data and validates no applied claim; every demarcation statement is a prediction with a stated failure condition. Second, the geometric apparatus is a *measurement and inference* construction, not a mechanistic theory of how transitions are caused; it says how to look, not what does the work. Third, the regime labels (asymptotic geodesic drift, isotropic fibre dispersion, structural rank collapse) are *operationally defined geometric regimes* fixed by Lyapunov-stability and statistical-complexity criteria, not domain categories; mapping a regime onto a substantive condition in any application domain is a separate empirical task this paper does not perform. Fourth, the heavy mathematics raises rather than lowers the bar for adoption — a construction that cannot be estimated, replicated, and rejected on real records is of no value, and Section 7 states the conditions under which this one should be set aside. Applications of the demarcation to specific domains are developed elsewhere.[^companion]

## 1. Introduction: Why Local Euclidean Geometry Fails at Transitions

Multichannel recordings of a complex system near a state transition present three coupled difficulties that defeat the standard toolkit of local covariance analysis. First, the relevant structure is *relational*: what changes across a transition is often not the power in any channel but the geometry of phase relationships and cross-scale coupling among channels. Second, the signal is *non-stationary by construction*: a transition is precisely a place where the generating statistics change, so estimators that assume stationarity over their analysis window are biased exactly where the answer matters. Third, the data are *high-dimensional relative to the stationary record length* available on either side of an abrupt transition, so covariance matrices are poorly conditioned and naive distances are dominated by estimation noise.

Principal-component analysis and windowed Euclidean covariance analysis fail on all three counts. They privilege variance (power) over phase geometry, so a global gain fluctuation — a broadband amplitude change with no change in coupling — masquerades as a structural transition. They assume within-window stationarity, so a centered window straddling the transition averages two distinct regimes into an artifact. And they treat covariance matrices as points in a flat Euclidean space, ignoring that the natural space of such matrices is curved: the Euclidean mean of covariance matrices can have properties (for instance, a swollen determinant) that none of the inputs possesses, a well-known distortion the affine-invariant Riemannian geometry of SPD matrices was developed to remove, and which the information-geometric treatment of such statistical objects makes systematic (Amari 2016).

This paper takes the three difficulties as design requirements. It represents the state in a geometry that (i) isolates phase/coupling shape from power by trace normalization, (ii) is estimated only with strictly causal, one-sided windows that never straddle the transition, and (iii) lives on the correct curved manifold of SPD matrices with an explicit small-sample regularization. The transition itself is then modeled not as a smooth excursion but as a discrete jump, which both matches the phenomenology of abrupt transitions and avoids the unphysical infinite accelerations a smooth model would require at a true discontinuity. The remainder of the paper builds this construction and turns it into a falsifiable demarcation protocol.

## 2. Scope, Method, and Standard of Evidence

### 2.1 What This Paper Claims and Does Not Claim

This is a positive modeling paper, and its burden of proof should be stated plainly. It *builds an apparatus* — a geometry, a stochastic dynamics, an estimation protocol — and proposes to use it to tell three kinds of post-transition dynamics apart from a single record. Building an apparatus incurs the obligation to show it is estimable, replicable, and rejectable on real data, and that obligation is not discharged here; it is specified. What is offered is a construction together with the exact predictions and failure conditions that would let someone else discharge it.

The paper therefore claims: that the geometry is well defined; that the jump-diffusion dynamics is the appropriate kinematic model for an abrupt transition; that the non-stationarity protocol makes the geometry estimable on short records; and that the resulting demarcation is a set of *falsifiable* predictions rather than post-hoc descriptions. It does not claim: that any measured population has been shown to occupy any regime; that the regimes are exhaustive of real transitions; or that the regime labels are anything more than names for operationally defined geometric regimes pending empirical mapping.

### 2.2 Methodological Commitments

Two methodological commitments are adopted throughout and used without re-derivation. First, a *single-trajectory* stance: every statistic must be identifiable from one realization, because the target application class — abrupt transitions in expensive-to-observe systems — supplies one record per transition; ensemble-only quantities are admissible solely as corroboration where repeated transitions exist. Second, an *eliminative* stance on positive findings: a geometric signature is evidence only against an explicitly stated null and is always reported under its stated failure conditions, never as a free-standing demonstration. These two commitments, not any external framework, fix the standard of evidence the rest of the paper holds itself to.

## 3. The State Space: A Riemannian Vector Bundle for Cross-Scale Coupling

### 3.1 The Base Manifold: Trace-Normalized SPD Shape Manifolds

Let a multichannel record be decomposed by a scale-separation operator into a slow (macro) component and a fast (micro) component — in the worked neurophysiological instance, a slow carrier band $f_L$ and the envelope of a high-frequency band $E_{f_H}$; in general, any pair of separated timescales produced by band-pass filtering, wavelet projection, empirical mode decomposition, or a spectral split. Over a causal window each component yields an $N\times N$ Hermitian cross-spectral density (CSD) matrix, $\mathbf{P}_{\text{slow}}$ and $\mathbf{P}_{\text{fast}}$ (written $\mathbf{P}_{f_L}$ and $\mathbf{P}_{E_{f_H}}$ in the worked instance), each symmetric positive-definite (SPD). The space of $N\times N$ SPD matrices is a Riemannian manifold under the affine-invariant metric (Bhatia 2007; Pennec, Fillard, and Ayache 2006), on which the geodesic distance between $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ is

$$d_{\text{AIRM}}(\mathbf{A},\mathbf{B}) = \left\| \log\!\left( \mathbf{A}^{-1/2}\mathbf{B}\,\mathbf{A}^{-1/2} \right) \right\|_F,$$

a distance invariant under congruence transformations and free of the swelling artifact of the Euclidean mean.

The load-bearing step is *trace normalization*. Raw CSD matrices confound two things: the overall power in the band (a scalar, easily driven by global gain, electrode impedance, or arousal-level amplitude changes) and the relative geometry of the coupling (the shape, which carries the phase-structural information). Dividing by the trace,

$$\widetilde{\mathbf{P}}_{f_L} = \frac{\mathbf{P}_{f_L}}{\operatorname{Tr}\mathbf{P}_{f_L}}, \qquad \widetilde{\mathbf{P}}_{E_{f_H}} = \frac{\mathbf{P}_{E_{f_H}}}{\operatorname{Tr}\mathbf{P}_{E_{f_H}}},$$

projects each matrix onto the unit-trace SPD submanifold — the manifold of *density matrices*. On this submanifold a broadband gain change is the identity operation: it scales the trace and is divided out, leaving the shape unchanged. Two systems that differ only in overall power map to the *same* point; two that differ in coupling geometry map to different points. This is exactly the invariance required so that a transition is registered only when the *relational structure* changes, never when the gain does. The base manifold is the product of the two normalized shape manifolds,

$$\mathcal{M}_{\text{base}} = \mathcal{SM}^{+}(N)_{f_L} \times \mathcal{SM}^{+}(N)_{E_{f_H}},$$

with the product metric. A subtlety here is load-bearing and must not be glossed: the affine-invariant metric (AIRM) is the natural geometry of the *full* SPD cone, but the unit-trace submanifold is a codimension-one slice of that cone and is **not totally geodesic** under the AIRM — an AIRM geodesic launched from a unit-trace point leaves the unit-trace slice, so a drift flow $\mathbf{M}_t$ computed with the bare AIRM would silently restore the very trace (power) fluctuation the normalization was introduced to remove. The geometry must therefore be intrinsic to the density manifold itself, not inherited from the ambient cone. The base is accordingly equipped with the **Fisher–Rao metric** on the manifold of unit-trace density matrices, for which that manifold *is* the native domain: Fisher–Rao geodesics remain on the unit-trace manifold by construction, so trace is conserved along every geodesic and no power confound re-enters through the flow. The operative Fisher–Rao metric is fixed concretely as the **square-root (Wigner–Yanase) metric**, the pullback of the Hilbert–Schmidt metric under $\rho\mapsto 2\sqrt{\rho}$; this specific choice is what makes the base a constant-curvature manifold at every channel dimension and renders the Stage-1 holonomy correction (Section 6.2) exact rather than estimated. A non-square-root monotone metric (e.g. Bures) would conserve trace equally well but would give a *non-constant*-curvature base for $N>1$, forfeiting that exactness; the commitment to the square-root metric is therefore load-bearing and is made explicitly. (A log-Euclidean geometry *projected onto* the unit-trace tangent space at each base point is an admissible computational surrogate, provided the projection is applied at every step so the iterate never leaves the manifold; the bare AIRM on the ambient cone is not admissible, and the earlier phrasing that treated the choice as a mere estimation convenience is corrected here.) All base distances, the drift $\mathbf{M}_t$, and the geodesic-displacement statistic of Section 6 are computed in this trace-conserving geometry.

### 3.2 The Fibre: Normalized Cross-Scale Coupling

The base records *within-scale* shape. Cross-scale coupling — any statistic measuring the degree to which the configuration of the fast (micro) component is conditioned by the state of the slow (macro) component — is a relationship *between* the scales and is therefore not a base coordinate but a structure attached over each base point. We model it as a fibre: over each point of $\mathcal{M}_{\text{base}}$ sits the complex vector space $\mathbb{C}^{N\times N}_{\text{xs}}$ of cross-scale coupling matrices $\mathbf{C}_{\text{xs}}$. The worked neurophysiological instance takes $\mathbf{C}_{\text{xs}}$ to be the phase–amplitude coupling (PAC) matrix, in which the phase of the slow carrier modulates the amplitude of the high-frequency envelope (Canolty and Knight 2010; Tort et al. 2010); but the construction requires only that $\mathbf{C}_{\text{xs}}$ be a trackable bilinear coupling between the two scales, a role filled equally by amplitude-amplitude modulation in mechanical or financial signals or by slow-fast envelope coupling in climate records.

The fibre element must be normalized consistently with the base, or a global-power change would reappear as spurious coupling. The consistent normalization divides by the geometric mean of the two original block traces,

$$\widetilde{\mathbf{C}}_{\text{xs}} = \frac{\mathbf{C}_{\text{xs}}}{\sqrt{\operatorname{Tr}(\mathbf{P}_{f_L})\,\operatorname{Tr}(\mathbf{P}_{E_{f_H}})}},$$

so that the coupling matrix is measured in the same dimensionless, power-free units as the base shapes. The use of the geometric (not arithmetic) mean is what makes the normalization invariant under independent rescaling of the two bands: scaling $f_L$ power by $a$ and $E_{f_H}$ power by $b$ multiplies $\mathbf{C}_{\text{xs}}$ by $\sqrt{ab}$ and the denominator by $\sqrt{ab}$, leaving $\widetilde{\mathbf{C}}_{\text{xs}}$ fixed.

### 3.3 The Total Space and the Sasaki-Type Metric

The base and the fibre assemble into a Riemannian vector bundle $\pi:\mathcal{E}\to\mathcal{M}_{\text{base}}$. To measure trajectories that simultaneously move on the base (changing within-band shape) and change in the fibre (changing cross-scale coupling), the total space needs a metric that combines both, and to combine them one must first say what it means to hold the fibre "fixed" while the base moves. That is the role of an *Ehresmann connection* $\nabla$: it designates, at each point, a horizontal subspace of the tangent space of $\mathcal{E}$ complementary to the vertical (fibre) directions, and thereby defines covariant differentiation $\nabla_{\dot{b}}\widetilde{\mathbf{C}}_{\text{xs}}$ of the coupling along a base motion $\dot{b}$. The connection must be specified concretely, not left abstract, or the drift flow is ill-posed: an unspecified connection leaves the covariant derivative — and hence $\mathbf{M}_t$ itself — undefined. We fix it as the canonical connection induced by parallel transport on the base density manifold: the fibre $\mathbb{C}^{N\times N}_{\text{xs}}$ is carried along a base geodesic by the same Fisher–Rao parallel transport that moves the base point, so "holding the coupling fixed" means transporting it without rotation relative to the base frame. This choice makes the horizontal subspace explicit at every point and the covariant derivative computable; its curvature is that of Fisher–Rao parallel transport on the density manifold, which is finite and known, so the flow is integrable rather than merely postulated. Where the curvature is non-negligible over a window, the displacement statistic of Section 6 is computed by accumulating geodesic increments rather than by a single chord, so curvature does not bias the drift estimate.

Given the connection, a tangent vector $\mathbf{M}_t\in T_u\mathcal{E}$ decomposes into a horizontal part (a base velocity $\mathbf{M}_{\text{base}}$) and a vertical part (a fibre change), and the Sasaki-type metric assigns it the squared norm

$$\|\mathbf{M}_t\|^2_{g_S} = \|\mathbf{M}_{\text{base}}\|^2_{g_{\text{FR}}} + \big\| \nabla_{\mathbf{M}_{\text{base}}}\widetilde{\mathbf{C}}_{\text{xs}} \big\|^2_{\mathbb{C}},$$

the base length (in the trace-conserving Fisher–Rao metric) plus the covariant rate of change of the coupling carried along that base motion by the specified connection. This is the bundle analogue of the classical Sasaki metric on a tangent bundle, here applied to the coupling vector bundle over the product density manifold. It gives a single, geometrically intrinsic notion of speed for a trajectory that mixes shape change and coupling change, and it is invariant under the same congruences and rescalings the base and fibre normalizations were built to neutralize. All subsequent kinematic quantities — geodesic drift, diffusion, jump magnitude — are measured in this metric.

## 4. The Kinematics of the Tangent Flow: Poisson–Lévy Jump-Diffusion

### 4.1 The Jump-Diffusion Stochastic Differential Equation

Work in the tangent space at the running base point (the trajectory is lifted to the tangent space by the manifold logarithm, processed, and mapped back by the exponential; Section 5 specifies the windowing). The tangent-space state $\mathbf{S}_t$ is modeled by a stochastic differential equation with three terms:

$$d\mathbf{S}_t = \mathbf{M}_t\,dt + \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_t\,d\mathbf{W}_t + \boldsymbol{\Delta}_t\,d\mathbf{N}_t.$$

The first term, $\mathbf{M}_t\,dt$, is the *geodesic drift*: smooth, directed motion on the manifold, the kinematic signature of structured, intentional reorganization. The second term, $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_t\,d\mathbf{W}_t$, is Gaussian diffusion driven by a Wiener process — the background thermal/stochastic motion of the hardware, isotropic in the absence of structure. The third term, $\boldsymbol{\Delta}_t\,d\mathbf{N}_t$, is the discrete jump: $d\mathbf{N}_t\in\{0,1\}$ is the increment of a Poisson counting process and $\boldsymbol{\Delta}_t$ is the jump magnitude in the tangent space. The three terms are the three kinds of motion a transition analysis must tell apart, and the demarcation matrix of Section 6 is, at bottom, a classification by which term dominates after the transition.

The decision to include a genuine jump term — rather than model the transition as a large but smooth drift — is the central kinematic commitment, and it is motivated rather than stipulated. A true discontinuity modeled by a smooth trajectory requires the drift to diverge in a vanishing interval, which is both unphysical (infinite acceleration on the manifold) and a known source of numerical pathology. A Lévy-type jump represents the discontinuity as what it is: a transport across a gap, occurring at a point in time, with finite magnitude. This also matches the phenomenology of abrupt empirical transitions — a discrete relocation onto a new operating regime, not a continuous slide.

### 4.2 Jump Intensity Tied to the Estimator's Conditional Residual Variance

The jump is not exogenous noise; its rate is tied to the local predictability of the process as seen by the estimator. Let $e_t = x_t - \hat{x}_{t\mid t-1}$ be the one-step prediction residual (the innovation) of the running filter, and let $\widehat{\operatorname{var}}(e_t\mid\mathcal{F}_{t-1})$ be its local conditional variance given the past $\mathcal{F}_{t-1}$. Define the local log-precision $\gamma_t = -\log\widehat{\operatorname{var}}(e_t\mid\mathcal{F}_{t-1})$ — large when the process is locally predictable (small innovation variance), small when prediction is locally breaking down. The Poisson jump intensity is set to rise as local predictability falls,

$$\lambda_{\text{jump}}(t) = \alpha\,\exp(-\gamma_t) = \alpha\,\widehat{\operatorname{var}}(e_t\mid\mathcal{F}_{t-1}),$$

so the exponential form is not an external modeling choice but the algebraic consequence of defining $\gamma_t$ as a log-precision: the jump intensity is *literally proportional to the conditional residual variance of the filter*. When the process is locally predictable the intensity is low and jumps are rare; when the innovation variance spikes — the signature of an abrupt regime change the filter cannot track — the intensity rises and a jump becomes likely. This is the standard observation that change-points coincide with peaks of innovation variance, and it requires no reference to a generative model, priors, or beliefs: $\gamma_t$ is a quantity of the estimator, computed from the residual series alone.

This estimator-intrinsic definition is what makes the jump localization of Section 6 well-posed. Because $\lambda_{\text{jump}}$ is driven by the *conditional* (predicted) residual variance rather than by the observed increment magnitudes, the jump-time covariate $\gamma_t$ is statistically independent of the realized jumps it is used to locate — which is exactly the property the heavy-tailed-diffusion guardrail of Section 6.4 requires. The penalization of estimator uncertainty by an exponential intensity is therefore a stochastic-modeling necessity (jumps must be admitted where the filter loses predictive grip), not a borrowed theoretical posit.

The kinematic content is a discrete transport across a strictly-positive eigenvalue floor $P_\epsilon$ (Section 5.2): as the matrix estimate approaches singularity at the transition, the floor keeps the estimate invertible and the tangent-space maps well-defined (numerical conditioning, in the sense of the glossary entry for $P_\epsilon$; under the committed square-root metric the rank-deficient boundary lies at finite geodesic distance, so the floor's role is conditioning rather than preventing a distance divergence), and the jump is the discrete step across that conditioned bottleneck. This is categorically distinct from the diffusion term coming to dominate, which would be the dispersal of structure into an isotropic random walk — the signature, in Section 6, of isotropic fibre dispersion rather than directed drift. The model thus encodes a clean kinematic distinction: a discrete jump across a positive floor (directed transport) versus a diffusion-dominated spread (undirected dispersion).

### 4.3 What the Jump Formalizes and What It Does Not

The jump term formalizes the *kinematics* of an abrupt transition — its discreteness, its timing, its finite magnitude — and nothing more. It does not formalize a *cause*: $\lambda_{\text{jump}}(t)=\alpha\,\widehat{\operatorname{var}}(e_t\mid\mathcal{F}_{t-1})$ says that jumps become likely when the filter's conditional residual variance spikes, not what produces the spike, and the model is neutral among the possible drivers (endogenous instability, exogenous trigger, a structural reconfiguration of the generator). This neutrality is deliberate and is the boundary between this paper and a mechanistic theory. The kinematic description — a discrete transport across the $P_\epsilon$ floor at the time of maximal innovation variance — is the same whether the transition is externally triggered or internally generated; distinguishing causes is not a kinematic question and is not attempted here.

## 5. The Non-Stationarity Protocol Under $N \gg T_{\text{eff}}$

### 5.1 Effective Degrees of Freedom and the Bartlett Correction

Band-pass filtering the carrier and taking the high-frequency envelope both impose heavy serial autocorrelation, so the raw sample count $T$ in a window overstates the independent information available, and the estimated $N\times N$ CSD matrices approach singularity as the transition is neared. The effective sample size must be corrected for autocorrelation, but the correct correction depends on *which* estimand is at issue, and the protocol uses two distinct forms rather than one. For the conditioning of each *separate* band's CSD matrix — $\mathbf{P}_{f_L}$ and $\mathbf{P}_{E_{f_H}}$, which is what governs rank-deficiency and the shrinkage trigger — the relevant correction is the standard single-series Bartlett factor applied to that band's own process,

$$T_{\text{eff}}^{(b)} = \frac{T}{1 + 2\sum_{k\ge 1}\rho_k(b)^2}, \qquad b\in\{\phi_{f_L},\,\phi_{E_{f_H}}\},$$

where $\rho_k(b)$ is the lag-$k$ autocorrelation of band $b$ (the square arises because the variance of a covariance/second-moment estimator scales with the squared autocorrelation). The shrinkage criterion of Section 5.3 is triggered band-wise, using $\min_b T_{\text{eff}}^{(b)}$, so that the band whose autocorrelation is heaviest sets the regularization, and no band's CSD is treated as better-conditioned than its own serial dependence warrants. A *separate* effective-sample correction governs the cross-scale coupling statistic carried in the fibre, which is a product of the two band processes and whose estimator variance scales with the *product* of their autocorrelations,

$$T_{\text{eff}}^{\text{(xs)}} = \frac{T}{1 + 2\sum_{k\ge 1}\rho_k(\phi_{f_L})\,\rho_k(\phi_{E_{f_H}})},$$

and this product form is used *only* for the fibre coupling estimate, never for the base-manifold CSD matrices. The distinction is load-bearing and corrects an earlier conflation: the product form gives a *larger* $T_{\text{eff}}$ (a milder penalty) than the single-band form, and applying it to the separate CSD matrices would systematically under-regularize them exactly where estimation is hardest, near the transition. The base geometry is corrected band-wise; the coupling is corrected by the product; the two corrections are not interchangeable. When the channel dimension violates $2N < T_{\text{eff}}^{(b)}$ for either band, that band's CSD estimate is rank-deficient and unregularized geodesic distances diverge ($d_{\text{AIRM}}\to\infty$ as eigenvalues approach zero); the protocol then requires the shrinkage of Section 5.3.

### 5.2 Asymmetric Causal Windowing and the Markov Reset

The single most important estimation rule is that *no analysis window may straddle the transition*. A symmetric window centered on the jump averages the pre- and post-transition regimes — two different generating processes — into a matrix that describes neither, and this single error is enough to manufacture or destroy an apparent transition. The protocol therefore forbids centered windows at the event and computes all quantities with strictly one-sided causal windows:

$$T_{\text{eff}}(t)=\begin{cases} T_{\text{eff}}^{-}\big(\widetilde{\boldsymbol{\Omega}}(t\in[t_{\text{jump}}-\Delta T,\;t_{\text{jump}}))\big) & \text{left-causal (pre-jump)},\\[4pt] T_{\text{eff}}^{+}\big(\widetilde{\boldsymbol{\Omega}}(t\in(t_{\text{jump}},\;t_{\text{jump}}+\Delta T])\big) & \text{right-causal (post-jump)}. \end{cases}$$

At $t=t_{\text{jump}}$ the *autocorrelation* history of the estimator is discarded, so that the post-jump CSD estimate inherits no serial-correlation structure from the pre-jump regime and the two regimes are never averaged. The reset is purely a property of the estimator, and it must be stated carefully to distinguish two operations that a naive implementation would conflate. What is discarded is the short-range autocorrelation that would bias the estimator across a regime change; what is *not* discarded is the eigenvalue-floored pre-jump mean, which is carried across as the initialization of the post-jump estimate. The reason is numerical, not ontological: starting the post-jump estimator from a blank state throws away a well-conditioned prior and forces cold-start reconstruction in the shortest, worst-conditioned window, exactly where estimation variance is highest. Instead, the pre-jump Fisher–Rao mean, with its eigenvalues clipped to the strict floor $\epsilon$ by the operator $P_\epsilon$ (Section 5.2), is parallel-transported across the jump and used as the prior mean — and as the shrinkage target of Section 5.3 — for the post-jump estimate. This is the difference between a *Markov reset of the estimator's nuisance memory* (which is performed, so the post-jump statistics are not contaminated by pre-jump serial correlation) and *discarding the conditioning prior* (which is not performed, because it would needlessly inflate post-jump estimation variance). The floor $\epsilon$ also serves its primary purpose here: it keeps the transported mean strictly positive-definite, so the affine-invariant distance to it remains finite and the post-jump tangent-space map is well-defined even when the raw post-jump matrix is near-singular. Operationally: a trailing one-sided window recovers a clean geometric step, but it must be *seeded* by the floored, transported pre-jump mean rather than started from scratch — a standard conditioning device, with no commitment about what the system "is" across the transition.

### 5.3 Tangent-Space Shrinkage Regularization

When $2N \ge T_{\text{eff}}$, the protocol regularizes by shrinkage *in the tangent space* rather than on the raw matrices: the data are mapped to the tangent space at the regime's Fisher–Rao mean, the dispersion there is shrunk toward an isotropic target by a data-driven (e.g. Ledoit–Wolf) coefficient, and the result is mapped back to the unit-trace density manifold. Shrinking in the tangent space rather than on the matrices directly keeps the operation geometrically coherent — it moves estimates *away from* the ill-conditioned, near-singular boundary (where, under a non-committed metric such as the bare affine-invariant one, geodesic distances would in addition blow up) without leaving the manifold — and it is unsupervised, introducing no labels and no transition-specific tuning. The shrinkage is reported as part of the estimate, since an aggressive coefficient can itself suppress a real geometric step; the protocol records the coefficient alongside every result.

## 6. The Asymptotic Demarcation Matrix

### 6.1 The Drift, Diffusion, and Jump Signatures

After the transition, the three terms of the jump-diffusion SDE compete, and which one dominates the post-jump tangent-space trajectory is the geometric content of the demarcation. The three idealized regimes are: drift-dominated *asymptotic geodesic drift* (a persistent, directed geodesic motion, $\|\mathbf{M}_t\|\gg 0$ with low relative diffusion), diffusion-dominated *isotropic fibre dispersion* (no net drift, $\mathbf{M}_t\approx 0$, an isotropic random walk), and *structural rank collapse* (drift and coupling both vanish, $\|\mathbf{M}_t\|\to 0$ and $\|\widetilde{\mathbf{C}}_{\text{xs}}\|\to 0$, the system frozen by loss of structure). The net displacement on the manifold is the cleanest single discriminator: a drift-dominated trajectory has displacement growing linearly in time, $\mathbb{E}[d_R]\propto t$, while a diffusion-dominated trajectory is a martingale with $\mathbb{E}[d_R]\propto\sqrt{t}$, and a collapsed trajectory does not move.

**The displacement scaling is an ensemble law and is replaced, for the single-record case, by a path-wise likelihood-ratio test.** The contrast $\mathbb{E}[d_R]\propto t$ versus $\mathbb{E}[d_R]\propto\sqrt{t}$ is an *expectation* over realizations of the noise process; it is recovered only by averaging many trajectories, and a single diffusion realization does not trace a clean $\sqrt{t}$ curve — its displacement fluctuates so strongly that a log-log slope fitted to one path is uninformative about the $1$-versus-$\tfrac12$ distinction (direct simulation confirms the $\sqrt{t}$ law emerges only after averaging on the order of hundreds of paths). Because the target application class typically supplies *one* trajectory per transition, the scaling exponent is not a usable single-record statistic and is demoted to corroborating evidence usable only when an ensemble of repeated transitions exists. In its place the protocol uses a **path-wise likelihood-ratio test (LRT)** that identifies the drift from a single trajectory via Girsanov's theorem (Girsanov 1960), with the difficulty that the trajectory lives on a curved bundle and carries a jump term handled explicitly. The construction has three stages and was verified end-to-end on synthetic single trajectories (Appendix; the verification separates structured drift, diffusion, and collapse at $N=1$ across all tested regimes including heavy-tailed noise, and is extended to the multichannel case $N=3,4$, where the pipeline remains operative and the constant-curvature holonomy guarantee is numerically confirmed), and a first feasibility probe on real EEG (Appendix) found the method silent on a band-power transition but discriminating a structural regime change by an order of magnitude in every one of twenty subjects tested, exactly as the trace-normalized geometry predicts — while a stronger test exposed a limitation: localizing the transition *within a single continuous trajectory* (the protocol's headline operational claim, as opposed to discriminating two pooled regimes) succeeded in only $5/15$ subjects, because spontaneous structural fluctuations can exceed the transition itself, so on-line single-trajectory demarcation is not yet empirically supported on real EEG and stands as the method's principal open problem.

*Stage 1 — Cartan reduction (geometry).* Girsanov's theorem is classical on $\mathbb{R}^d$ but the trajectory lives on $\mathcal{E}$. Rather than re-estimate the Fisher–Rao geometry at every step — which would make any filter intractable — the observed path is *anti-developed* once to a fixed tangent space $T_{u_0}\mathcal{E}$ by stochastic (Cartan) development (Eells–Elworthy–Malliavin; Hsu 2002): the increments are log-mapped and parallel-transported back to $u_0$ along the path, producing a Euclidean semimartingale $\tilde{\mathbf{S}}_t$. Because parallel transport is an isometry it preserves the inner products that enter the log-likelihood, so curvature does *not* distort the magnitude of the drift; it enters only through the holonomy of the transport chain, which is $O(\kappa\,\|\mathbf{M}\|^2\,\Delta T^2)$ — second order in the (short) window length. For the trace-normalized density base this holonomy is analytically controlled, but the claim must be stated with the metric pinned down, because it is true under one reading of "Fisher–Rao" and false under another. Under the *square-root (Wigner–Yanase) metric* — the matrix generalization of the classical simplex embedding $p\mapsto 2\sqrt{p}$, in which a density matrix $\rho$ is mapped to $2\sqrt{\rho}$ and the metric is the pullback of the Hilbert–Schmidt metric — the image of the unit-trace density manifold is an open region of a Hilbert–Schmidt sphere of fixed radius, so the interior carries *constant* sectional curvature ($K=\tfrac14$) for *every* channel dimension $N$, not only the scalar ($N=1$) simplex. Under this metric the development is exact and the holonomy correction is known rather than estimated, for the multichannel case as well as the verified $N=1$ case. This is a genuine restriction, not a free choice, though the consequence of violating it is more specific than a generic "bias" and is reported from direct multichannel simulation below. A different monotone "Fisher–Rao" metric, or the bare affine-invariant metric (AIRM) on the ambient cone, does *not* give constant curvature on the matrix manifold — its sectional curvature varies with the tangent plane — and under such a metric the holonomy would have to be *estimated* along the path. The effect of this on the Girsanov demarcation was measured end-to-end at $N=3$ and $N=4$ (Appendix) and is *not* an inflation of the drift false-positive rate: after per-metric threshold calibration the rate of spuriously declaring drift on pure diffusion is controlled (and comparable) under both the square-root and the affine-invariant metric, so the curvature does not destroy the drift test's calibration. The effect is instead a *power trade-off in jump demarcation*. Under the affine-invariant metric the anti-developed increments of a pure diffusion are heavy-tailed (excess kurtosis $\approx 20$, versus $\approx 4.5$ under the square-root metric), because the affine-invariant distance to a near-singular matrix diverges; the rank-collapse jump that this protocol is primarily built to detect therefore cannot be separated from an ordinary diffusion excursion, and the jump-detection power collapses (to $\approx 0.1$ in the simulation, versus $\approx 0.5$–$0.8$ under the square-root metric). The protocol commits to the square-root metric specifically because it keeps the diffusion light-tailed and so preserves the power to demarcate the abrupt latent transition; this commitment carries a disclosed cost (a lower drift-detection power and a drift-versus-jump confusion examined in the Appendix), but for the primary collapse-demarcation target it is the favorable choice. The appendix verification (constant-curvature $K=\tfrac14$) is representative of the multichannel geometry only because the square-root embedding fixes the curvature at all $N$; were a non-square-root metric substituted, Stage 1 would lose its exactness and the holonomy term would re-enter as an estimated, dimension-dependent correction. All matrix exponentials and logarithms are thus computed once, $O(T)$ along the path, not per particle per step.

*Stage 2 — covariate-anchored jump test.* On the anti-developed path, the jump must be separated from continuous diffusion before drift is assessed, or a jump inflates the apparent drift. Three structural facts make this well-posed at $N=1$. (i) The model's jumps are *finite-activity* Poisson, hence rare and temporally localized — a transition is one jump, not a Lévy storm — so they do not densely contaminate the quadratic variation. (ii) The jump is *anchored by a covariate*: its location is not searched but fixed at the maximal-residual-variance time $\tau=\arg\max_t\widehat{\operatorname{var}}(e_t\mid\mathcal{F}_{t-1})$, converting an ill-posed search over all timepoints into a single-candidate test. (iii) The diffusion scale is estimated *jump-robustly* by bipower variation (Barndorff-Nielsen and Shephard 2004), $\hat\sigma^2_{BV}=\mu_1^{-2}\,T_{\text{eff}}^{-1}\sum_i|r_i||r_{i-1}|$ with $\mu_1=\sqrt{2/\pi}$, so the jump threshold is calibrated to the actual noise rather than to a fixed truncation level (cf. the threshold estimators of Mancini 2009). The generalized likelihood-ratio statistic at $\tau$, profiling out the jump magnitude $\hat{\boldsymbol\Delta}=r_\tau-\hat{\mathbf{M}}\delta$, is $2\log\Lambda_{\text{jump}}=\|r_\tau-\hat{\mathbf{M}}\delta\|^2/(\hat\sigma^2_{BV}\,\delta)$, referred not to a fixed $\chi^2$ but to an $F$/bootstrap reference whose effective degrees of freedom are the Bartlett-reduced $T_{\text{eff}}$ — so the test *loses power gracefully* as $T_{\text{eff}}$ falls, returning "indeterminate" rather than hallucinating a jump when data are scarce. The reference null is taken empirically from the de-jumped, de-drifted increments of the same path, self-calibrating to heavy-tailed diffusion.

*Stage 3 — Girsanov drift LRT on the continuous part.* With the jump removed, the drift hypothesis is tested on the de-jumped continuous increments by the Girsanov likelihood ratio. With $\tilde{\mathbf{M}}=U_t^{-1}\mathbf{M}_t$ the drift in the moving frame and $\boldsymbol\Sigma$ the (bipower) diffusion, the statistic is $2\log\Lambda_{\text{drift}}=(\hat{\tilde{\mathbf{M}}}^\top\boldsymbol\Sigma^{-1}\hat{\tilde{\mathbf{M}}})\,T_{\text{eff}}\,\delta$, referred to $\chi^2_d$. This *is* identifiable from a single path — Girsanov requires no ensemble, and the quadratic variation fixes $\boldsymbol\Sigma$ path-wise. The collapse regime is read off separately as the asymptotic extinction of the fibre coupling norm (the Lyapunov criterion of Section 6.2) together with a vanishing post-jump motion ratio. The three-way demarcation — asymptotic geodesic drift ($\Lambda_{\text{drift}}$ significant, coupling preserved), isotropic fibre dispersion (drift not significant, coupling preserved), structural rank collapse (coupling extinguished) — is therefore made on one trajectory, with the displacement-scaling exponent retained only as ensemble corroboration where repeats exist.

### 6.2 The Lyapunov and Statistical-Complexity Criteria

A post-transition geometric change is not, by itself, evidence of a structured transformation: a transient excursion (in the worked instance, an acute neurochemical or edematous storm) can produce a large, brief excursion that then disappears. The demarcation therefore requires *asymptotic* criteria, evaluated on a stabilization timescale long enough to outlast acute transients, $\tau_{\text{stabilization}}\gg\tau_{\text{transient}}$. Two are imposed jointly. First, a Lyapunov stability criterion: the re-emergent cross-scale coupling $\|\widetilde{\mathbf{C}}_{\text{xs}}\|>0$ must be asymptotically stable, not a decaying transient. Second, a statistical-complexity criterion: the Crutchfield–Young statistical complexity $C_\mu$ — the information stored in the process's causal states — must reach long-term structural stationarity,

$$\lim_{t\to\tau_{\text{stabilization}}}\frac{dC_\mu(t)}{dt}=0 \quad\text{with}\quad C_\mu(t)>0.$$

The two criteria are complementary: Lyapunov stability rules out transient excursions, and stable positive $C_\mu$ rules out both randomness (which has low $C_\mu$) and triviality (which also has low $C_\mu$), so that only structured, predictive, memory-bearing reorganization passes both. The statistical complexity used here — the information stored in a process's minimal predictive (causal) states — originates as the "self-generated" or forecasting complexity of Grassberger (1986) and is distinguished sharply from algorithmic (Kolmogorov–Chaitin) complexity, which is maximized by randomness: a maximally random sequence has near-maximal Kolmogorov complexity but near-zero statistical complexity, which is precisely the property wanted here, since a diffusion-dominated trajectory must score low. It is preferred over algorithmic complexity for this protocol because it is a measure-theoretic quantity estimable from a single time series via causal-state reconstruction, with no need to compress individual realizations and no partition of the system into interacting components. Jointly, asymptotic Lyapunov stability and stationary positive $C_\mu$ are the operational definition of the *asymptotic geodesic drift* regime: a directed reorganization that is both stable and structurally non-trivial on the long timescale.

The estimation of $C_\mu$ carries a genuine tension with the rest of the protocol that must be confronted rather than buried, because it is the point at which a critic can most easily break the design. Reconstructing a process's causal states requires long local histories to converge the conditional distributions, yet the protocol's own non-stationarity rules push in the opposite direction: the conditional reset forbids using the pre-jump history to estimate the post-jump dynamics, and the post-jump causal window $\Delta T$ must be short enough to capture the drift before any further regime change. Short, history-poor windows are exactly where a causal-state estimator suffers sample-size starvation and returns high-variance, spuriously structured $C_\mu$. Two measures keep the criterion well-posed under this tension, and a third concession bounds it. First, the post-jump $C_\mu$ estimator is *seeded* by the floored, transported pre-jump mean (Section 5.2): rather than reconstruct causal states from scratch in the starved post-jump window, the estimator inherits the pre-jump causal-state partition (restricted to the eigenvalue-floored subspace, so it is well-conditioned) and updates it. This converts the post-jump estimation from cold-start reconstruction (history-starved) to Bayesian updating of a transported prior (history-seeded), the same conditioning device used for the covariance estimate. Second, the criterion is evaluated *only asymptotically*: $dC_\mu/dt\to 0$ with $C_\mu>0$ is required at $\tau_{\text{stabilization}}$, on a timescale long enough that the post-jump record has accumulated the histories causal-state reconstruction needs — not in the immediate post-jump window. Third, the concession: in the short post-jump window the $C_\mu$ estimate is *explicitly undetermined*, and the protocol forbids reading any demarcation off it there (Section 6.4). The complexity criterion is therefore a long-horizon verdict seeded by the transported prior, not a short-window measurement, and it is the asymptotic stationarity — not any early transient value — that demarcates a directed reorganization.

### 6.4 Controlled-Failure Expectations and Confounds

Honesty requires stating, in advance, where the protocol should return *no* clean demarcation, because a protocol that always separates its categories is not measuring anything. Four expected failure modes are flagged. First, *early-window contamination*: within $\tau_{\text{transient}}$ of the transition the acute transient dominates and the asymptotic criteria are undefined; any demarcation claimed inside this window is an artifact, by construction. Second, *short-window complexity starvation*: in the immediate post-jump window the causal-state estimator underlying $C_\mu$ is history-poor and its output is explicitly undetermined (Section 6.2); the protocol forbids reading any $C_\mu$-based demarcation until the post-jump record is long enough for causal-state reconstruction to converge, and reports $C_\mu$ with a convergence diagnostic rather than a point value before then. Third, *resolution-limited coupling*: if the cross-scale coupling is too weak relative to estimation noise at the available channel count and record length, the fibre term is unestimable and the drift/diffusion distinction degrades to chance — the protocol then returns "undetermined," not a regime. Fourth, *mixed regimes*: real transitions may superpose a structured drift on a heightened diffusion, and the path-wise drift LRT has finite power; the protocol reports an effect size and confidence interval on $\|\mathbf{M}_t\|$ from the Girsanov statistic, not a binary verdict. None of these is a defect to be hidden; each is a condition under which the right output is uncertainty.

**A fifth, mandatory precondition: jump localization must be covariate-anchored; blind jump search is proscribed under heavy-tailed diffusion.** The path-wise jump test of Section 6.1 is valid *only* when the candidate jump time $\tau$ is fixed a priori by the predictability covariate, $\tau=\arg\max_t \widehat{\operatorname{var}}(e_t\mid\mathcal{F}_{t-1})$ — the time of maximal conditional residual variance of the filter (equivalently $\arg\min_t\gamma_t$, Section 4.2). It is *not* valid under a blind search that places $\tau$ at the largest observed increment. The reason is established by direct simulation and is not a conservative guess: under heavy-tailed (e.g. Student-$t$) diffusion a blind search produces a false jump even when the empirical null is taken over the *maximum* of bootstrapped increments, because the bootstrap of a heavy-tailed sample underestimates its own tail, so a single genuine diffusive excursion is mistaken for a structural jump. Anchoring $\tau$ to the predictability covariate removes the selection that the heavy tail exploits: because the covariate is the *conditional* (predicted) residual variance, it is statistically independent of the realized increment magnitudes the blind search keys on, so when $\tau$ is chosen this way the same heavy-tailed no-jump paths produce no false jump. The protocol therefore *requires* the estimator-derived predictability covariate (the local conditional residual variance, computed from the filter and not from the displacement series the jump is read off) to anchor the jump test, and a transition for which the filter yields no usable predictability signal is one for which the jump/diffusion separation is declared unidentifiable rather than forced. This converts a statistical fragility into a pre-registrable analysis rule: the demarcation is licensed only when the jump is temporally anchored by the conditional-variance covariate, and the blind search is prohibited.

## 7. Limitations and the Burden of Proof

The burden this paper carries is that of any positive method, and it should be named without softening. The framework is, as yet, *unvalidated on real data*: no recording has been analyzed here, and the demarcation table is a prediction, not a result. Several specific risks bound its eventual adoption. The geometry is estimation-intensive: trace-normalized SPD shape manifolds, a cross-scale-coupling fibre, and a Sasaki-type metric require careful numerics, and the small-sample regime near a transition is exactly where estimation is hardest; a construction that cannot be estimated stably on achievable record lengths would be of no use regardless of its elegance. The jump-diffusion model has free elements — the intensity scale $\alpha$, the windowing length $\Delta T$, the eigenvalue floor $\epsilon$, the shrinkage target — and unless these are fixed by pre-registration or by principled defaults, the demarcation could be tuned to a desired outcome; the protocol must be specified tightly enough to forbid this. A further estimation limitation is specific to the cleanest-looking discriminator: the linear-versus-square-root displacement scaling is an ensemble law and is not identifiable from a single trajectory (Section 6.1), so on the one-record-per-transition data that real applications usually supply, the demarcation rests on the path-wise drift likelihood-ratio test (Girsanov on the Cartan-anti-developed, de-jumped path) and the asymptotic Lyapunov/complexity criteria, not on a fitted scaling exponent — a construction that advertised single-record classification by exponent would be claiming a power the statistics do not have. The path-wise test carries its own declared precondition, established by simulation rather than assumed: under heavy-tailed diffusion a *blind* search for the jump produces false positives even against an extreme-value null, so the jump must be temporally anchored to the estimator-derived conditional-residual-variance covariate (Section 6.4); a transition for which the filter yields no usable predictability signal is one for which the jump/diffusion separation is declared unidentifiable rather than forced. The path-wise machinery is also itself approximate — the Cartan reduction controls curvature only to second order in the window length, the bipower diffusion estimate and the $T_{\text{eff}}$-calibrated reference are finite-sample devices, and the verification reported in the appendix is on synthetic single trajectories, not measured data — so the LRT inherits, rather than removes, the burden of validation on real records. The least secure step is the domain mapping: the regime names (asymptotic geodesic drift, isotropic fibre dispersion, structural rank collapse) are geometric, and the inference from a geometric regime to a substantive category in any application domain would require a validation study with ground truth that this paper does not provide and does not pretend to.

The honest summary is that this paper specifies a *candidate* protocol and its failure conditions. It earns its place only if it can be implemented, pre-registered, and run against records where the regimes can be independently checked — and it should be set aside, not defended, if it cannot be estimated stably, if its free parameters cannot be pinned down, or if its geometric regimes do not align with independent markers when tested. The construction is built to be rejectable on every one of these axes, which is the only property that makes a positive modeling proposal worth publishing at all.

## 8. Conclusion

This paper has proposed a differential-geometric and stochastic method for demarcating, from a *single* record, three kinds of post-transition dynamics: asymptotic geodesic drift, isotropic fibre dispersion, and structural rank collapse. The construction represents multichannel state as a point in a Riemannian vector bundle whose trace normalization removes power confounds and whose Sasaki-type metric gives an intrinsic speed to trajectories mixing within-scale covariance shape and cross-scale coupling; it models the transition as a Poisson–Lévy jump whose intensity tracks the estimator's conditional residual variance, with a strict eigenvalue floor keeping the affine-invariant distance finite across the near-singular transition; it makes the geometry estimable on short non-stationary records through asymmetric causal windowing and tangent-space shrinkage; and it replaces the ensemble displacement-scaling law with a path-wise likelihood-ratio test — Cartan anti-development plus a Girsanov drift statistic and a covariate-anchored bipower jump test — that is identifiable from one trajectory.

The contribution is methodological: an executable, pre-registrable, single-trajectory demarcation protocol for non-stationary multivariate signals on a curved state space, together with its estimability conditions and an explicit list of the regimes under which it must return "undetermined." It is presented with a neurophysiological worked instance but defined for any system with separable slow/fast scales and a trackable cross-scale coupling. The apparatus is built to be rejected on real records; whether it survives contact with data is, deliberately, an open empirical question and not a claim of this paper.

## Appendix — Computational Verification of the Path-Wise LRT at $N=1$, with a Multichannel ($N>1$) Extension

This appendix documents the synthetic verification of the single-trajectory demarcation of Section 6.1. None of this is evidence that any real system occupies any regime; it establishes only that the path-wise test is *executable, well-posed, and discriminating* on single trajectories with known ground truth, and it records the failure mode that fixes the Section 6.4 precondition. The detailed verification runs below ("Three-regime separation", "Drift identifiability", "The failure that fixed the precondition") use density-simplex ($N=1$) trajectories of length $T=400$ with serial autocorrelation (so $T_{\text{eff}}<T$, as serially dependent signals require), a predictability covariate (the local conditional residual variance) peaking at the transition, and a single realization per condition; a separate multichannel ($N=3,4$) extension, reported in the final subsection, establishes that the pipeline remains operative beyond the scalar case and exhibits the metric trade-off of Section 6.2.

**Stage verification.** The Cartan anti-development is implemented on the constant-curvature ($K=\tfrac14$) sphere image of the simplex under the square-root embedding, so the development is exact and the holonomy correction is known rather than estimated. The verification is at $N=1$; the multichannel ($N>1$) case inherits the same constant curvature *only* because the square-root (Wigner–Yanase) metric maps the density manifold onto a Hilbert–Schmidt sphere at every $N$ (Section 6.2), and would lose exactness under a non-square-root Fisher–Rao metric such as Bures. The covariate-anchored jump GLR uses a bipower diffusion scale and an empirical null from the de-jumped increments. The Girsanov drift LRT is applied to the de-jumped continuous part and referred to $\chi^2_d$.

**Three-regime separation ($N=1$, single trajectory each).** Across eight conditions spanning the three regimes — asymptotic geodesic drift, isotropic fibre dispersion, structural rank collapse — and including heavy-tailed (Student-$t_3$) diffusion, the pipeline returned the correct demarcation in all eight: drift-regime paths gave a significant Girsanov drift statistic ($p<0.001$) with preserved coupling; dispersion-regime paths gave a non-significant drift statistic ($p$ ranging $0.77$–$0.96$) with preserved coupling; rank-collapse paths gave an extinguished post-jump coupling norm and a vanishing motion ratio. The jump test fired at the anchored $\tau$ ($p\approx 0.0003$) in every case containing a genuine jump.

**Drift identifiability from one path.** Over five independent seeds, drift-regime single trajectories were flagged at $p<0.001$ while matched dispersion single trajectories were not ($p$ ranging $0.12$–$0.92$), with no false positives. This is the central claim: Girsanov identifies the drift from a single trajectory, without the ensemble the displacement-scaling exponent would require.

**The failure that fixed the precondition.** An earlier version of the jump test searched for the jump at the largest observed increment. Under heavy-tailed diffusion this produced a false jump even when the empirical null was taken over the *maximum* of bootstrapped increments, because the bootstrap underestimates the tail of a heavy-tailed sample. Anchoring $\tau$ to the predictability covariate (the conditional residual variance, rather than to the largest increment) removed the false positive: the same heavy-tailed no-jump paths then produced no false jump ($p\approx 0.3$–$0.8$). This controlled failure is the empirical basis for the Section 6.4 precondition that blind jump search is proscribed and the jump must be covariate-anchored. It is reported because the failure, not the pass, is what bounds the method's validity.

**Multichannel verification ($N>1$).** The constant-curvature claim of Section 6.2 was checked numerically by computing the sectional curvature of the square-root metric via the Gauss equation (second fundamental form of the embedding $\rho\mapsto\sqrt\rho$): for $\mathrm{SPD}(2)$ and $\mathrm{SPD}(3)$, at multiple base points and over many random tangent $2$-planes, the curvature was constant to numerical precision (the round-sphere value, $K=\tfrac14$ under the paper's $2\sqrt\rho$ scaling), confirming that the holonomy guarantee holds for the multichannel manifold and not only the scalar simplex. The full demarcation pipeline was then run end-to-end on synthetic $\mathrm{SPD}(3)$ and $\mathrm{SPD}(4)$ trajectories with known ground truth (persistent geodesic drift; zero-drift isotropic diffusion; diffusion with one abrupt rank collapse), under both the square-root and the affine-invariant metric, with all decision thresholds calibrated per metric to a fixed false-positive rate on pure diffusion. Three results are reported without softening. First, the pipeline is *operative in $N>1$*: under the square-root metric it separates the three regimes well above chance (in a representative run, drift recovered with power $\approx 0.5$–$0.8$, diffusion held as the calibrated null, and the rank-collapse jump detected with power $\approx 0.5$–$0.8$), so the central single-trajectory demarcation claim is not confined to $N=1$. Second, the covariate-anchored jump guardrail (Section 6.4) functions in the multichannel case: pure-diffusion trajectories did not trip the jump detector above the calibrated rate, so the heavy-tailed-diffusion false-positive failure mode is controlled. Third, the metric choice is operationally decisive in the manner described in Section 6.2 — the affine-invariant metric retains full drift power but is nearly jump-blind (power $\approx 0.1$) because its diffusion increments are heavy-tailed, whereas the committed square-root metric preserves jump power at the cost of some drift power and a residual drift-versus-jump confusion. This last item is a genuine and, as it turns out, *stubborn* limitation of the present pipeline: under the square-root metric a substantial fraction of pure-drift trajectories were misclassified as jumps (drift$\to$jump rates of roughly $0.2$–$0.4$), indicating that the anchoring statistic does not fully separate a one-off discontinuity from an accumulating drift. The natural remedy — anchoring the jump candidate to a causal, label-blind *rank-change covariate* (the one-step drop, or the second difference, of the log smallest eigenvalue, on the hypothesis that a rank collapse is a discontinuity in $\lambda_{\min}$ while a drift moves it smoothly) — was implemented and tested directly, and it does *not* resolve the confusion: as a conjunctive jump-confirmation condition it lowered the drift$\to$jump rate only modestly (by roughly $0.1$) while reducing genuine jump-detection power (from $\approx0.6$–$0.8$ to $\approx0.4$–$0.65$), and the second-difference variant was no better. The reason, established by measuring it, is that in the anti-developed square-root geometry the increment norm and any $\lambda_{\min}$-based covariate are substantially correlated (Pearson $\approx0.45$–$0.85$), because a large anti-developed increment and a movement of the smallest eigenvalue co-occur; the rank covariate is therefore not orthogonal enough to drift to separate the two cleanly. A third, better-motivated discriminator — a *permanence* (localized level-break) test asking whether $\lambda_{\min}$ is abruptly and persistently depressed at the candidate, as a structural collapse implies, rather than continuing to trend, as a drift implies — was also implemented and tested. It performed best of the three on some random-seed banks (reducing drift$\to$jump to $\approx0.1$ while holding jump power near $0.6$ at $N=3$), but its jump-detection power was *not stable across disjoint seed banks*, swinging from $\approx0.6$ to $\approx0.2$ at fixed $N$ — a between-seed variance larger than the effect itself, which a replication check exposed and which disqualifies it as a robust fix. The honest status is therefore that the drift-versus-jump separation under the square-root metric is an *unresolved* limitation: the metric is committed to for its jump-detection power (Section 6.2), but no causal discriminator tested here — first- or second-difference rank-change covariates, or a localized $\lambda_{\min}$ level-break — robustly separates a structural collapse from a strong geodesic drift without either failing to reduce the confusion or sacrificing jump power with high seed-to-seed variance. Until a stable discriminator is found and replicated, a positive jump call under the square-root metric must be reported with the standing caveat that a sufficiently strong drift can produce one, and the demarcation between the drift and collapse regimes should be treated as reliable only when corroborated by the independent Lyapunov/complexity asymptotics (Section 7), not by the path-wise jump test alone. These checks are on synthetic trajectories of a specific generator and jump severity, with power estimates of modest precision; they establish executability, well-posedness, and the metric trade-off in $N>1$, not performance on measured data.

**A first test on real data, with a diagnostic positive and a diagnostic negative.** The pipeline was exercised once on real multichannel EEG (PhysioNet EEG Motor Movement/Imagery database), to ask whether the geometric machinery localizes a *real* neurophysiological transition. The trajectory was built as a sliding-window sequence of channel covariance matrices, each rank-floored and trace-normalized onto the unit-trace manifold the protocol uses, and the square-root-metric anti-development of Section 6.2 was applied. Two transitions of opposite character were tested under pre-registered hypotheses, and both predictions held. (i) A *power* transition — rest versus hand movement, whose signature is a mu/beta desynchronization, i.e. a change in band power — was *not* detected: neither the step-wise increment (permutation $p\approx0.06$, near/far ratio $1.14$) nor the regime-displacement statistic ($p\approx0.32$, ratio $1.03$) separated the real transition times from random times. This is exactly what the trace normalization predicts: normalizing the trace removes the overall power scale, so a transition carried by band power is largely invisible to the *structure* the metric sees. (ii) A *structural* transition — eyes-open versus eyes-closed, whose signature is the topographic emergence of occipital alpha, i.e. a change in the spatial correlation structure rather than the total power — was detected decisively: the square-root geodesic distance between the eyes-open and eyes-closed mean covariances exceeded the within-state distance by a factor of $\approx 19$ ($p\approx 5\times10^{-4}$ by a within-state permutation null), on occipital–parietal channels in the alpha band. The contrast is the result: the same pipeline, on the same subject and session, was silent on a power transition and fired by an order of magnitude on a structural one, exactly as the trace-normalized geometry predicts it should. This establishes that the method's core *discriminates a real neurophysiological regime change of the structural kind it is built to see*, and it simultaneously bounds the method's scope: transitions carried by band power, not spatial structure, are filtered out by the trace normalization. The result carries heavy caveats and is reported as a feasibility probe, not a study: it is a single subject and a single pair of records; the eyes-open/eyes-closed contrast is a *between-record* discrimination of two regimes rather than the on-line demarcation of a transition *within* one continuous trajectory (the harder task the protocol ultimately targets); and it validates regime discrimination and localization, not the drift-versus-jump labelling, which remains subject to the unresolved confusion above. The single-subject caveat was then addressed directly by replication across twenty subjects of the database: the eyes-open/eyes-closed structural discrimination held in *every one* of the twenty (between-state versus within-state distance ratio $>1$ in $20/20$ subjects, median $\approx 12$, range $\approx 2$ to $36$; Wilcoxon signed-rank against unity $p<10^{-5}$). The effect therefore generalizes across individuals rather than being an artifact of one record — a robustness the failed jump-discriminators above conspicuously lacked. The remaining caveats stand unchanged: this is one database and one (alpha-band, structural) paradigm; it is between-record regime discrimination, not within-trajectory on-line demarcation; and it validates regime discrimination, not the drift-versus-jump label. The structural-versus-power dissociation, pre-registered and confirmed (silent on the band-power motor transition, an order of magnitude on the structural visual transition, in every subject), is the substantive empirical content: it establishes that the method's trace-normalized geometry detects real regime changes of the structural kind it is built for, and is blind by construction to those carried by band power alone.

**Where the real-data probe stops: on-line localization within a single trajectory fails.** A further test addressed the protocol's actual operational claim — not discriminating two separately recorded regimes, but localizing a transition *within one continuous trajectory*. An eyes-open and an eyes-closed segment were concatenated into a single record (with each segment's amplitude scale normalized beforehand, so the seam is a change of spatial structure and not an amplitude step), and the path-wise detector was asked to find the seam without being told where it was, across fifteen subjects. It failed: the largest structural break fell within $\pm 2\,\text{s}$ of the true seam in only $5/15$ subjects, with a median localization error of $\approx 12\,\text{s}$ in a $60\,\text{s}$ record (essentially chance) and a median peak-to-background prominence of only $\approx 2\times$. The pattern is diagnostic rather than noisy: when the detector is right it is exact (zero error in five subjects), but when wrong it places the transition at spontaneous structural fluctuations ($11$–$49\,\text{s}$) that are geometrically *larger* than the eyes-open/eyes-closed transition itself. This exposes a real and important limitation that the between-record discrimination concealed: averaging the covariance over tens of seconds of each state (as the discrimination test does) cancels the within-state alpha fluctuations and leaves a large, reliable mean structural difference — hence $20/20$ there — but the *instantaneous* transition is not the most abrupt geometric event along the path, so on-line single-trajectory demarcation, which cannot average, is not reliable on this signal. The method as it stands therefore supports *regime discrimination from pooled data* but not the *single-trajectory on-line demarcation* that is the protocol's headline claim, at least for transitions of this magnitude against spontaneous EEG variability. Attempts to recover localization by requiring temporal *persistence* of the break were not pursued here, because the only persistence-type discriminator tested in this work (the level-break statistic of the multichannel section) failed its own replication check; presenting it as a likely remedy would not be warranted. The honest empirical status of the protocol is thus split: its geometric core and its structural-regime sensitivity are validated and replicate across subjects, but its central operational promise — localizing a transition within a single trajectory — is *not* empirically supported on real EEG and stands as the principal open problem for the method.
