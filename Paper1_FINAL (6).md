## Abstract

This paper makes a single negative claim and defends it with maximal severity: the inherited apparatus of cybernetics and optimal control is logically incompetent to describe existential transformation — metanoia, conversion, sanctification — without annihilating the agency of the subject it claims to transform. The argument is transcendental rather than constructive. It does not propose a new mechanism of transformation; it proves that the dominant family of mechanisms cannot so much as formulate the question.

The paper proceeds in three movements. First, it reconstructs the conditions any account of persistence under transformation must satisfy, using a diagnostic taxonomy (R/P/M/Lambda) that names the failure modes of persistence — dissolution, rigid repetition, and motion without retention — against aperiodic memory-preserving continuity. The identity-relevant functional *I* is presented not as a physical property discoverable in a dynamical flow but strictly as a *pragmatic-indexing metamodel*: a consistency contract that binds whoever claims that an identity persisted to a determinate set of geometric constraints.

Second, and centrally, the paper establishes the **Trilemma of Cybernetic Impossibility**. Control-theoretic accounts of conversion face three jointly exhaustive failures. The Paradox of Endogenous Tautology shows that change generated by internal rules alone (Ashby's law of requisite variety and ultrastability) is the execution of a latent subroutine, not conversion: the system changes state precisely in order not to change organization. The Paradox of Exogenous Annihilation shows that coercive trajectory-tracking control forces the agency eigenvalues to negative infinity and zeroes adaptive diffusion: to hold a system on a prescribed trajectory by penalizing deviation, the controller must, in the perfect-tracking limit, kill the agent — finite-gain forcing instead pays a graded, monotone agency cost (whereas a mere trigger that releases a transition toward a latent attractor is a distinct, non-excluded case). The Paradox of Incommensurability shows that optimization algorithms collapse at axiological discontinuities where the cost function itself is replaced, generating singularities and indeterminacy in backward induction (Bellman collapse). The space these three paradoxes leave empty is exactly the space a non-coercive transformation would have to occupy.

Third, the paper identifies the residual admissibility profile — Class G — that survives all three exclusions, distinguishes it categorically from a unilateral integrity-bounded transition (the U-limit) by their divergent post-jump topology rather than by adjectival shielding, and gives sanctity a precise reading in the vocabulary of the Free Energy Principle: the limit in which the precision weight on destructive policy priors vanishes while the entropy of the total policy-search space remains high and intact.

The paper does not prove theology, the soul, or the existence of grace. It proves that classical control science lacks the tools even to ask whether such transformation is possible.

**Two delimitations of the impossibility claim, stated at the outset because the rest of the paper depends on them.** First, the result is *conditional on a definition of control*. The trilemma's exhaustiveness is not a discovery about the space of all conceivable algorithms; it follows from a substantive premise — that to be a control account *at all* is to define directed change relative to a held-fixed evaluative structure (a cost, a reward, an enumerated family of these, or a fixed higher-order objective). That premise is defended in section 7.5 — and for the most-pressing objection, the case of endogenous preference dynamics in which the agent revises its own value structure, it is upgraded from a defended premise to a proved one: a Meta-Optimization Collapse Theorem shows that an autonomous preference dynamics on a compact value space falls into a trichotomy (gradient descent on a meta-potential, unbounded dispersion, or bounded recurrence), none of which realizes conversion, so that conversion is necessarily *externally occasioned* — and that external occasion is the excluded coercion horn only if it is deviation-penalizing tracking, the non-tracking trigger remaining the admissible Class-G profile. The premise remains contestable at its definitional root: a reader who holds that open-ended, novelty-driven, or value-base-mutating optimization is still "control" will read the result as a claim about a narrower family. The honest form of the thesis is therefore: *given that control is evaluation relative to a fixed evaluative point, conversion — the transformation of that point — cannot be formulated as control.* Second, the impossibility is *exact only in the high-dimensional, self-organizing regime*. As the companion paper concedes without hedging, a low-dimensional system whose identity/agency mode decomposition is supplied in advance by a designer is trackable by a multi-objective controller; the irreducibility argued here is a property of systems in which that decomposition must be carved out by the system's own viability-constrained dynamics, against a value structure genuinely novel rather than pre-enumerated. The unqualified word "impossibility" in the title is shorthand for this doubly-conditioned claim, and is used in that sense throughout.

**Keywords:** persistence under transformation; transcendental critique; cybernetics; optimal control; Ashby's law; requisite variety; Bellman equation; dynamic programming; metanoia; conversion; personal identity; philosophical theology; grace; acedia; resurrection; Parfit; relational identity; Lambda-regime persistence; Class G; free energy principle; precision weighting; active inference; agency preservation.

**Architectural orientation.** The center of this paper is the negative result of section 7. The R/P/M/Lambda taxonomy supplies diagnostic scaffolding: it names the failure modes and the target regime of persistence. The Trilemma supplies the destructive core: it proves that the three available control-theoretic strategies for moving a system between regimes each fail catastrophically when applied to a subject whose agency must be preserved. Class G is then not advanced as a discovered mechanism but identified as the *shape of the hole* the three paradoxes leave — the admissibility profile any non-coercive transformation must satisfy, stated as a type signature rather than a causal law. The theological sections are disciplined analogical applications of this negative grammar; their burden is to show that the structures theology has always insisted on (grace that neither coerces nor is self-generated, sanctity that frees rather than rigidifies) are precisely the structures classical control cannot render. The paper's unity lies in a single transcendental question: what must any adequate account of transformation preserve, given that the dominant scientific account of directed change provably destroys it?

**Scope and non-claims.** Four delimitations hold throughout and are stated once here. First, the formalism is a diagnostic typing and exclusion device, not a deductive apparatus: its conditions exclude cases, they do not generate theorems, and Class G in particular is an admissibility predicate, not a causal mechanism. Second, the theological sections are analogical applications of the grammar, not derivations of doctrine from dynamics; no theological claim is licensed by the formalism, and the grammar's non-neutrality — its fit with participatory rather than strongly monergistic accounts — is a declared delimitation, not a hidden result. Third, the central argument is negative: it does not show how conversion happens; it shows that a specific and dominant family of models cannot formulate it without self-contradiction. Fourth, the use of free-energy and active-inference vocabulary in section 10.1 is a modeling choice for a theological-phenomenological structure, not a claim that sanctity is a neurocomputational process; the apparatus is borrowed to state an exclusion criterion precisely, not to reduce holiness to inference.

**A note on terminology.** Several terms in this paper carry more rhetorical charge than their licensed content, and the reader should discount accordingly. "Impossibility" means the doubly-conditioned claim of the abstract (relative to a definition of control, in the high-dimensional regime), not absolute physical or logical impossibility. "Transcendental" marks the *form* of the argument (a claim about conditions of possibility of a kind of description), not a Kantian deduction. "Annihilation" is shorthand for the specific limit $D_{ag}\to 0$, not a metaphysical claim about destruction. "Category error" (used of the naive Mori–Zwanzig reading in the companion paper) and "non-holonomic constraint" (used of an eigenframe rotation there) are analogies that the companion paper itself later qualifies. Where these terms appear, they are load-bearing only in their defined senses; their ordinary connotations are not part of any claim.

## 1. General Introduction

A recurring problem across metaphysics, psychology, and theology is persistence under transformation: how can something remain recognizably itself while undergoing real change? Three errors frame the problem by negation. The first equates identity with perfect invariance — if something changes, it is no longer itself. The second equates identity with unrestricted flux — if anything can change, nothing constrains what persists. The third equates identity with copyable information — if the same pattern is reproduced, the same person continues. Each error is coherent in isolation; each fails on examination.

But naming the failure modes is only the diagnostic half of the problem. The constructive half — and the one that exposes a deep inadequacy in the dominant scientific vocabulary for directed change — concerns *transition*: granting that there is a target regime of healthy persistence, how does a subject move toward it from a dispersive or disordered regime? The natural answer, in an age whose default model of directed change is cybernetic, is *control*: identify the desired state, specify a cost or error signal, and drive the system toward it by feedback or forcing. This paper's central thesis is that this answer is not merely incomplete but *logically incompetent* for the specific case of existential transformation. The control paradigm cannot describe metanoia — conversion, the radical reordering of a person — without, in the very act of describing it, destroying the agency that makes the person a person rather than a mechanism.

This is a transcendental claim in the Kantian sense: it concerns the conditions of possibility of a certain kind of description. It does not say that conversion is impossible. It says that *the control-theoretic framework cannot formulate conversion as conversion*. The three available strategies — endogenous self-correction, exogenous forcing, and cost-function optimization — each collapse when applied to a subject whose identity-bearing trajectory and agentive openness must both survive the transformation. The collapses are not contingent engineering difficulties; they are structural, and section 7 proves them as such.

The argument requires precise vocabulary, and the paper builds it in stages. Sections 3 through 6 develop the diagnostic taxonomy and the identity functional, with one crucial reframing relative to naive dynamical accounts: the functional *I* is not treated as a property one could in principle measure in a flow. It is treated as a *pragmatic-indexing metamodel*, a consistency contract. Section 7 states and proves the Trilemma of Cybernetic Impossibility. Section 8 identifies Class G as the residual profile the trilemma leaves empty. Sections 9 through 12 apply the resulting negative grammar to theology — grace, sanctity, acedia, resurrection — always analogically and always to show that what theology has insisted on is exactly what classical control cannot render. Section 13 draws the conclusion.

**The argument in condensed form.** *The problem.* A subject can change radically and remain itself, but only some changes preserve it. The question of transition is: by what kind of intervention can a subject be moved toward healthy persistence without being either left to its own devices or overpowered?

*The diagnostic scaffolding.* R names dissolution by disorder; P names rigidity by repetition; M names dispersal by motion without retention; Lambda names aperiodic, memory-preserving persistence. The taxonomy is purely diagnostic and indexed to a pragmatic contract *I*.

*The negative core.* The three control-theoretic strategies for moving a subject between regimes each fail. Endogenous change is tautological (Ashby): the system changes to stay organized. Exogenous control is annihilating: it kills agency to impose a trajectory. Optimization is incommensurable: it is undefined where the cost function itself changes. These are the three horns of the Trilemma of Cybernetic Impossibility.

*The residual.* Class G is the admissibility profile that survives all three exclusions — externally sourced (not endogenous), receptivity-mediated (not coercive), mode-selective (not totalizing), and aperiodic (not collapsing into optimization toward a fixed target). It is the shape of the space the trilemma leaves empty, stated as a type signature.

*The theological applications.* Grace is the participatory transformation that classical control cannot model; the U-limit and Class G are distinguished by post-jump topology, not by adjective; sanctity is precision collapse on destructive policy priors with total policy entropy intact; acedia is the Class M failure of the post-origin field; resurrection-by-copying fails the non-branching constraint. All analogical.

*The conclusion.* You are not, this paper argues, watching someone try to prove a miracle with equations. You are watching a proof that classical control science lacks the instruments even to state the problem.

## 2. Scope, Method, and Formal Status

This article belongs to the genre of programmatic critique in the philosophy of science. It does not present a complete mathematical classification, a finished metaphysics of the person, a proof of doctrine, or an empirical theory of consciousness. It should not be evaluated as a contribution to formal control theory in the engineering sense, nor as dogmatic theology. It offers a *transcendental negative result*: a demonstration that a dominant family of models cannot formulate a certain problem, together with the diagnostic vocabulary needed to see why.

The argument proceeds by diagnostic reconstruction and impossibility argument. First, it identifies the persistence-failure modes. Second, it fixes the identity functional as a contestable contract rather than a measured property. Third — the core — it proves that each control-theoretic strategy for transition fails when both identity and agency must be preserved. Fourth, it characterizes the residual admissibility profile. Fifth, it tests the negative grammar against theological stress cases.

The formal notation disciplines distinctions and states exclusions; it does not claim new theorems in dynamical systems. The framework operates at three levels, each independently evaluable.

| Level | What is claimed | What is not claimed |
|---|---|---|
| Diagnostic schema | R, P, M, and Lambda are useful persistence-relevant distinctions under chosen observables and timescales. | The classes exhaust all dynamical regimes or constitute a complete classification. |
| Transcendental claim | The control-theoretic paradigm cannot formulate existential transformation without collapsing into tautology, annihilation, or incommensurable indeterminacy. | A positive mechanism of conversion is supplied. |
| Theological analogy | Grace, acedia, sanctification, and resurrection can be clarified by a grammar of transformation without erasure, and are precisely what classical control cannot render. | Doctrine is derived from dynamics. |

**Failure conditions of the critique.** The negative argument itself can fail, and naming how is part of its discipline. It would fail if: (i) some control-theoretic strategy could be exhibited that moves a subject toward Lambda-like persistence while provably preserving both $D_{id} < D_c$ and $D_{ag} > 0$ and surviving an axiological cost-function change — collapsing one of the three horns; (ii) the constitutive/indicative distinction collapsed under domain pressure, so that "preserving agency" had no determinate content; or (iii) the three horns turned out not to be jointly exhaustive, leaving a fourth control strategy unexamined. The critique earns its place by exposing these conditions rather than hiding them.

### 2.1 What a Negative Critique Claims and Does Not Claim

A transcendental negative critique establishes a limit, not a positive theory. The analogy is to impossibility results elsewhere in science: a proof that perpetual motion violates thermodynamics does not tell you how to build an engine, but it permanently rules out a class of attempts and redirects inquiry. This paper's result has the same structure. It does not tell anyone how conversion occurs. It rules out, with structural rather than empirical force, the attempt to model conversion as control. What remains after the ruling-out is not a mechanism but a *constraint on any adequate mechanism* — the admissibility profile of section 8.

One comparison must be disclaimed precisely because the previous paragraph invites it. Calling this an impossibility result "in the genre of the limitative theorems" is an analogy of *form*, not a claim of formal kinship, and the distinction is load-bearing. The structure at issue here — a system that must transform the very evaluative point relative to which its operations are defined — has a surface resemblance to the self-reference at work in the classical limitative results (Gödel's second incompleteness theorem, Tarski's undefinability of truth, Löb's theorem) and to the undecidability of non-trivial semantic properties of programs (Rice's theorem) and its descendants in the theory of self-modifying and successor-verifying agents. That resemblance is real and may be worth pursuing, but this paper does *not* establish, and does not assume, any formal reduction to those results: it specifies no provability predicate, fixes no formal system, and proves no undecidability. The trichotomy of section 7.5 is a dynamical-systems argument about autonomous flows on a compact value space, not a metamathematical one, and its force does not derive from any of the named theorems. Where the resemblance points — whether agency-preserving conversion is an instance, a generalization, or merely a structural echo of internal-successor-verification limits — is an open question for future work and is flagged as such, not imported as support. Readers should therefore treat the limitative-theorem comparison as motivational framing for the *kind* of claim being made (a permanent ruling-out rather than an empirical difficulty), and not as a borrowed proof; the argument stands or falls on section 7, independently of any connection to formal incompleteness or undecidability.

The critique is therefore valuable in proportion to the dominance of the paradigm it limits. Cybernetics, optimal control, reinforcement learning, and predictive-processing-as-control are the default scientific languages for directed change in the twenty-first century. To show that this entire family cannot so much as state the problem of non-coercive transformation is to clear the ground on which any positive account must be built.

This raises a fair objection about the status of the paper's mathematical notation, which should be answered before it accumulates. A reader may protest that the paper imports heavy notation — $\lambda_\parallel$, $\lambda_\perp$, $D_{ag}$, $D_{id}$, Koopman spectra — yet disclaims producing theorems, and that this is a convenient evasion: notation borrowed for authority while exempted from the rigor notation normally carries. The objection presupposes a false dichotomy — that mathematical writing is either engineering-style theorem-proving or else mere metaphor. The notation here does neither and claims neither. It functions as an *a priori syntactic limitation tool*: it specifies, in exact form, the boundary conditions any control model would have to satisfy to formulate agency-preserving transformation, and the argument then demonstrates that those conditions cannot be jointly satisfied. The claim $\lambda_\parallel \to -\infty$ is an instance: it is not decoration and not a theorem of control engineering, but the precise specification of the asymptotic behavior brute exogenous control must impose to guarantee a trajectory — a condition stated sharply enough that its incompatibility with the agency condition $D_{ag} > 0$ can be exhibited rather than asserted. The paper presents no deductive proof and apologizes for none; the formalism does the more limited work of fixing the conditions precisely and showing their co-satisfaction to be impossible. That is the whole of its mathematical ambition, and it is calibrated to exactly that.

### 2.2 The Identity Functional as a Pragmatic-Indexing Metamodel

The single most important methodological commitment of this paper is the demotion of the identity-relevant functional *I* from a putative physical property to a *pragmatic-indexing metamodel*. Naive dynamical accounts of identity are tempted to treat persistence as something one could read off a trajectory: inspect the flow, measure the correlations, and discover whether identity is preserved. This paper rejects that temptation explicitly, because it is the source of the circularity that makes such accounts vacuous.

On the present account, *I* is not detectable in the flow. It is a contract entered into by an observer — a clinician, an analyst, a theologian — who proposes to make a persistence claim. The contract has the following force: *if you assert that this system persisted as the same X, then you are committed to indexing the following geometric and dynamical constraints as the ones whose violation would refute your claim.* The constitutive/indicative partition is, on this reading, a heuristic-taxonomic choice of the observer, not a fact about the system. It says which features the observer's claim treats as load-bearing and which it treats as free to vary.

This demotion is not a weakness; it is the precondition for non-circularity. Because *I* is a contract fixed in advance rather than a property fitted after the fact, it can be violated — the observer can be shown to have committed to constraints the system fails to meet — and it can be contested by other observers who propose a rival contract. The thesis that identity questions are well-formed only relative to such a contract places the framework within the sortal-dependence tradition: "same?" is always shorthand for "same *what*?", and *I* formalizes the *what* (Wiggins 2001). The departure from a purely sortalist account is that *I* may index a practice or a memory-bearing process rather than a substance-kind, and that it carries a dynamical vocabulary (the correlation-power condition, the constitutive/indicative split) the bare sortal leaves unspecified.

The danger this guards against is circularity. If *I* were chosen after inspecting the trajectory, any pattern could be made persistent by selecting the contract that preserves it. The three locks of section 4.1 — domain justification prior to analysis, adversarial failure cases specified before classification, and independent contestability by experts not committed to the verdict — are what keep the contract honest. The claim "this system is Lambda-like" therefore never means "this system intrinsically instantiates Lambda." It means: relative to a pre-specified, externally contestable contract, the system retains the relevant correlation power over the declared timescale. Change the contract and the classification may change. This is not a defect; it is the epistemic discipline that makes a persistence claim falsifiable.

## 3. Identity as Trajectory, Not State

Throughout this article, remaining the same self does not mean retaining every property, memory, or psychological disposition. It means preserving a historically indexed *trajectory* through admissible change. A copied pattern, even if structurally identical, is not by that fact the same trajectory. This non-branching requirement is essential because fission, teletransportation, and uploading cases show that psychological or informational continuity can ramify (Parfit 1984; Lewis 1976; Williams 1970; Agar 2016).

A living organism persists while exchanging matter and energy. A person persists through memory revision, moral transformation, trauma, sleep, illness, and social re-description. The state of the system at any instant is not the carrier of its identity; the carrier is the continuity of the path, indexed by the contract *I*. Two consequences follow. First, identity is a four-dimensional notion: it concerns the shape of a history, not the contents of a moment. Second — and this is what the trilemma will exploit — any intervention that moves the system must be evaluated by what it does to the *trajectory and its openness*, not merely by what end-state it produces. A controller that reaches the target state by collapsing the path's future openness has not transformed the subject; it has replaced a trajectory with a terminus.

## 4. The Identity-Relevant Functional *I* as a Consistency Contract

The functional *I* is specified by a domain, an observable $f$, a tolerance $\epsilon$, and a timescale $\tau$. The quadruple $(I, f, \epsilon, \tau)$ is the content of the contract. It does not describe the flow; it commits the observer.

### 4.1 The Three Locks Against Circularity

A valid use of *I* requires three locks. First, **domain justification**: the contract must be motivated by the persistence question before any trajectory is inspected. Second, **adversarial failure cases**: the observer must specify, in advance, the transformations that would count as identity-destroying. Third, **independent contestability**: domain experts not committed to the target verdict must be able to challenge the contract. A contract that cannot be challenged is not a contract; it is a tautology.

### 4.2 Constitutive and Indicative Invariants as a Heuristic Partition

The contract partitions features into constitutive and indicative. A *constitutive* invariant is one whose destruction the contract treats as breaking the trajectory: its loss is replacement, not change. An *indicative* invariant tracks identity but may change without, on the contract, destroying the subject. The partition is heuristic and observer-relative: it expresses which features the persistence claim treats as load-bearing. It is also externally contestable — a critic can argue that a proposed constitutive invariant is in fact merely indicative, or vice versa. The partition does no work as a discovery about the system; it does all its work as a structuring of the claim.

### 4.3 Worked Applications and Adversarial Failure Cases

The discriminating power of *I* is shown by cases it handles and, crucially, by attempted contracts that *fail*.

**Moral conversion** preserves constitutive invariants — non-branching trajectory, responsibility-bearing history, practical agency — while reorganizing indicative ones — habits, desires, self-description. On a well-formed contract for personhood, this is transformation, not replacement.

**Teletransportation/fission** preserves copyable structure but breaks the non-branching trajectory. A contract for singular numerical personhood must, by its adversarial cases, classify this as identity failure despite pattern-continuity.

**A too-permissive contract.** Consider $I_{profile} = $ similarity of memory reports, personality profile, and behavioral dispositions. It tracks many features ordinarily used to recognize persons, and a broad tolerance permits learning and aging. Yet it fails in the fission case: if two successors each satisfy $d_{I_{profile}}(S, A) \le \epsilon$ and $d_{I_{profile}}(S, B) \le \epsilon$, then both would count as continuations, violating non-branching. The failure is not that $I_{profile}$ tracks irrelevant features; it tracks relevant *indicative* ones. Its error is mistaking indicative invariants for constitutive ones. The contract is rejected as too permissive.

**A too-restrictive contract.** Symmetrically, $I_{exact} = $ complete preservation of all memories, dispositions, and self-descriptions classifies moral conversion, trauma recovery, and ordinary maturation as identity-destruction. It treats indicative invariants as constitutive and is rejected as too restrictive.

These failures clarify the contract's role: it must be strict enough to reject branching copies and permissive enough to allow genuine transformation. A contract that cannot satisfy both under adversarial testing is not admissible for the domain. This is the substance of external contestability.

## 5. A Diagnostic Taxonomy of Persistence: R, P, M, and Lambda

The taxonomy classifies persistence-relevant behavior relative to a contract. It is not a set of mutually exclusive boxes for all systems; real systems combine noise, rhythm, chaos, and long-memory organization at different scales. A system behaves as R, P, M, or Lambda only relative to the question: which features must remain identifiable, over which timescale, under which transformations?

### 5.1 Class R: Random Disorder

Class R denotes regimes in which order is overwhelmed by stochastic disorder. Such systems may preserve transient structure but cannot reliably maintain identity-bearing memory. The failure mode is dissolution: the pattern is scrambled or erased. In theological analogy, R can describe dissolution into unstructured flux; the analogy is controlled, since R is a formal regime, not a moral category.

### 5.2 Class P: Mechanical Periodicity

Class P denotes strict periodicity, $x(t+T) = x(t)$, $T > 0$. It preserves structure by repetition, resisting dissolution by *closing* the space of transformation. Its entropy rate is zero in the strict case; it contains no new history. If identity were repetition, the perfect identity would be a loop. Living identity requires continuity through difference. In theological analogy, P corresponds to mechanical legalism or dead ritualization — order without responsiveness. The pathology is mechanical closure, where repetition replaces response.

### 5.3 Class M: Mixing-Chaotic Order

Class M is essential because not all failure is random or rigid. A Class M system is deterministic, bounded, and dynamic, with positive maximal Lyapunov exponent $\lambda_{max} > 0$ and decaying correlations $C_f(t) \sim e^{-\beta t}$, $\beta > 0$. Nearby trajectories separate exponentially and identity-relevant correlations vanish. This is motion without retention: energetic but dispersive. Waddington's epigenetic landscape makes the contrast vivid — a canalized trajectory persists through difference because the landscape retains its organizing valleys; Class M is that landscape flattened, in which the system still moves but no valley returns it to its course (Waddington 1957). Modern attention economies — algorithmic feeds, perpetual notification, rapid affective switching — illustrate the regime: they do not merely distract a pre-existing subject but may shape it into a pattern of motion without memory. In ascetic theology, this structure illuminates acedia (section 11).

### 5.4 Class Lambda: Aperiodic Memory-Preserving Persistence

Lambda is not a new mathematical class of dynamical systems. It is a persistence-relevant diagnostic regime: bounded, deterministic, non-mechanical, aperiodic, memory-preserving dynamics relative to a specified contract $(I, f, \epsilon, \tau)$. The same physical process may count as Lambda relative to one contract and as M relative to another; this observer-relativity is the correct epistemic structure for a persistence claim.

For a mean-zero observable $f$, define the autocorrelation under the flow $C_f(t) = \langle f, U_t f \rangle$, with $U_t$ the Koopman evolution, and the correlation-power average $P_f(T) = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T |C_f(t)|^2\,dt$. A system preserves the relevant pattern if $\liminf_{T\to\infty} P_f(T) > 0$. For finite applications this is replaced by a pre-specified finite-window estimate over the relevant timescale, validated against phase-randomized surrogates so that a windowed estimate inseparable from its surrogates does not license a Lambda attribution. The condition distinguishes Lambda from Class M: both may be deterministic, bounded, and non-periodic, but M loses correlation power while Lambda retains it. This tracks the spectral dichotomy: an observable with non-trivial projection onto the point spectrum of $U_t$ yields almost-periodic, power-retaining correlations (Lambda-like and P), whereas an observable in the absolutely continuous spectrum has $C_f(t)\to 0$ by Riemann–Lebesgue (Class M) (Walters 1982; Halmos 1956). The same separation has an information-theoretic reading: in computational mechanics, memory-preserving structure shows substantial excess entropy on the identity-relevant observable while remaining aperiodic — neither random nor merely repeated — and is quantified by the statistical complexity of the process's causal states (Crutchfield and Young 1989; Crutchfield 2012).

Crucially, the correlation-power condition is *necessary but not sufficient*, and is not the locus of the distinction. Long-memory processes also retain correlation power. What separates Lambda from a long-memory process is the relation between the flow and the contract *I*: a long-memory process retains power on *some* observable, whereas Lambda requires retention on the *specific* observable the contract designates as carrying a constitutive invariant. The dynamical vocabulary fixes the genus; the contract fixes the species. A critic who shows two systems sharing every dynamical signature yet differing in contract-relevant invariant has not refuted Lambda; they have illustrated why the indexing to *I* is load-bearing and the dynamics alone are not.

| Related concept | Similarity to Lambda | Key difference |
|---|---|---|
| Quasi-periodicity | Structured aperiodicity with organized frequency content. | May not involve contract-relevant memory or the constitutive/indicative distinction. |
| Metastability | Temporary stability under perturbation. | May describe transient rather than persistent organization. |
| Attractor dynamics | Organized resistance to perturbation. | Can freeze agency by reducing the system to a low-dimensional basin. |
| Long-memory processes | Temporal retention and slow decay of correlations. | May retain statistically without preserving the invariant the contract makes constitutive. |

## 6. Persistence Is Not Stasis

Class P makes vivid why persistence and stasis must be distinguished. A periodic process preserves form by repeating it, but personal, moral, and spiritual persistence cannot be reduced to repetition. A person incapable of growth, repentance, or new understanding has not achieved perfected identity; such a person has entered mechanical closure.

The taxonomy R/P/M/Lambda now names the space of persistence-relevant regimes. R dissolves the subject. P freezes it. M disperses it through motion that cannot remember. Lambda names the regime in which the subject neither dissolves, freezes, nor forgets. But the taxonomy is *purely diagnostic*. It classifies what is happening to a system; it cannot say how a system moves from one class to another without losing what matters. A system in Class M does not spontaneously enter Lambda; a system in Class P does not spontaneously recover openness.

This is the threshold at which the central question of the paper appears — and at which, the paper will argue, the dominant scientific answer collapses. The intuitive answer to "how does a system move toward Lambda?" is *control*: specify the target, drive the system there. Section 7 proves that control, in each of its three forms, either is no transformation at all (it merely re-expresses the system's own dynamics), or achieves transition by destroying the agent, or cannot even be defined across the axiological discontinuity that conversion essentially involves.

## 7. The Trilemma of Cybernetic Impossibility

### 7.1 The Central Problem: Why Control Theory Cannot Formulate Conversion

The diagnostic taxonomy poses a transition problem: move a subject from a dispersive regime (M), a rigid regime (P), or a disordered regime (R) toward Lambda-like persistence. The default scientific framework for directed transition is control theory in its broad sense — cybernetics, optimal control, dynamic programming, and their descendants. This section proves that this framework cannot formulate the transition that conversion names, because conversion is defined by two constraints the framework cannot jointly honor: the identity-bearing trajectory must be preserved ($D_{id} < D_c$) *and* the agency-bearing openness must be preserved ($D_{ag} > 0$), across a transformation that genuinely reorders the subject rather than merely relocating it within a fixed value structure.

The framework offers exactly three strategies for producing transition. The change can be generated from within the system's own dynamics (endogenous control / feedback). It can be imposed from outside by force on the state (exogenous control / forcing). Or it can be computed as the solution to an optimization over a value or cost function (optimal control / dynamic programming). These three exhaust the directed-change repertoire of the cybernetic tradition, from its foundational statement (Wiener 1948) through its modern reinforcement-learning descendants (Sutton and Barto 2018). The trilemma shows that each of the three fails, and that the three are jointly exhaustive of the control paradigm. What remains — the conjunction of properties no control strategy can deliver — is the admissibility profile of section 8.

Let the subject's dynamics, near the identity manifold $M_\Lambda$, be decomposed into a transverse identity-bearing subspace $E_{id}$ (whose uncontrolled diffusion destroys identity) and a tangential agency-bearing subspace $E_{ag}$ (whose variation constitutes adaptive openness): $TM_\Lambda = E_{id} \oplus E_{ag}$. Conversion requires contraction of destructive transverse deviation, $\lambda_\perp < 0$, with *protected* tangential openness, $\lambda_\parallel = 0$ (neither frozen nor dispersed), and the diffusion conditions $D_{id} < D_c$, $D_{ag} > 0$. The trilemma is that no control strategy can establish this profile.

### 7.2 The Paradox of Endogenous Tautology (Ashby's Closure)

The first strategy generates the change from within: the system's own feedback reorganizes it toward the target. Call the internal vector field $F_0$, so that the transformative term lies in $\langle F_0 \rangle$, the span of the system's own dynamics.

The paradox is that endogenously generated change is not conversion but the execution of a latent subroutine. Ashby's analysis of ultrastable systems is decisive here. An ultrastable system is one equipped with a mechanism that changes its own parameters whenever an essential variable leaves its viable bounds, until the variable is returned to those bounds (Ashby 1960). The law of requisite variety states that such a system can absorb disturbance only to the extent that its regulator has at least as much variety as the disturbance (Ashby 1956). The combined upshot is structural: an ultrastable system *changes state precisely in order to preserve its organization*. The parameter change is in the service of homeostatic closure. Whatever new behavior appears was already a reachable configuration of the system's own variety — a latent subroutine, selected by the disturbance, deployed to restore the prior organization.

This is the formal negation of conversion. Conversion is the change of the organization itself — the reordering of the field of value, the replacement of one cost structure by another (section 7.4). Endogenous change cannot deliver this, because by construction it operates to *keep the organization fixed*. Formally: if the transformative field lies in $\langle F_0 \rangle$, then the "new" trajectory is a trajectory the system's own dynamics already admitted; the system has moved within its existing organization, not transcended it. The change of state masks the conservation of organization. In the theological register, this is the structure of self-salvation: the creature reorganizes itself, calls the result transformation, and has in fact only redeployed its own latent variety to preserve its prior closure. Endogenous control is therefore excluded not because it fails to produce change, but because the change it produces is, by Ashby's analysis, organization-preserving and hence not conversion.

### 7.3 The Paradox of Exogenous Annihilation (The Death of Agency)

The second strategy imposes the change from outside, by direct force on the state. The relevant case must be specified precisely, because "external input" is far too broad: what the second horn concerns is *coercive trajectory-tracking control* — a control architecture in which an input $u_t$ drives the system along a prescribed path and asymptotically penalizes deviation from it, overriding the system's own dynamics wherever they would depart from the commanded route. It is this class, and only this class, that the paradox excludes. Inputs that merely trigger or release a transition — that push the system through a bifurcation toward an attractor already latent in its own dynamics, without thereafter penalizing departures from a commanded path — are a different category entirely and are treated separately below; they are not what the second horn rules out.

The paradox is that brute trajectory-tracking control achieves the target trajectory only by killing agency. Consider what it takes to *hold* a system on a prescribed trajectory by vector force in the presence of the system's own dynamics and noise. To make the imposed trajectory dominate, the controller must suppress the tangential degrees of freedom that would otherwise carry the system away from the prescribed path. In the linearized picture, this means driving the tangential (agency) eigenvalues strongly negative — in the limit of perfect tracking, $\lambda_\parallel \to -\infty$ — so that any agentive excursion is instantly contracted back onto the commanded trajectory. Equivalently, the agency-bearing diffusion is suppressed: $D_{ag} \to 0$. The commanded path is realized, but the subspace $E_{ag}$ that constitutes the subject's openness, exploration, and capacity for self-originated variation has been collapsed. The annihilation is a property of *deviation-penalizing tracking* specifically: it is the penalty on departure from the commanded route that forces the tangential contraction.

This is annihilation in the only sense relevant to identity-bearing systems. The subject reaches the target *state*, but as a contracted point with no tangential freedom — a Class P closure imposed from outside. The trajectory has become a terminus; the agent has become a mechanism executing a command. The condition $D_{ag} > 0$ that conversion requires is precisely the condition trajectory-tracking control must violate to guarantee the commanded path. One cannot hold an agent on a prescribed trajectory by force and leave it free to depart from it; the freedom to depart is exactly what the tracking penalty must suppress. In the theological register, this is coercion: the creature is moved but does not participate, because participation *is* the tangential openness the controller had to kill. Trajectory-tracking control is therefore excluded not because it fails to produce the target state, but because producing it by deviation-penalizing force requires $D_{ag} \to 0$, which is the destruction of the agent.

One refinement keeps this horn from overclaiming. The collapse $D_{ag}\to 0$ is the *perfect-tracking limit* (zero asymptotic tracking error, infinite gain). Real forcing is finite-gain: a controller that holds the state within a bounded error tube — for instance funnel control or prescribed-performance control, which guarantee a prescribed error bound with *finite*, state-dependent gain (Ilchmann, Ryan, and Sangwin 2002; Bechlioulis and Rovithakis 2008) — leaves $\lambda_\parallel$ finite and $D_{ag}>0$. The honest statement of the horn is therefore graded, not binary: deviation-penalizing forcing reduces the agency-bearing diffusion *monotonically* with the tightness of the imposed bound, annihilating it only in the limit of exact tracking. "Annihilation" names that limit; for finite bounds the relevant quantity is the *agency cost* of forcing, which is strictly positive and increasing in the control authority exerted. The theological reading — coercion as the death of participation — is the limit point of a continuum of partial coercions, each of which suppresses, in proportion to its authority, exactly the tangential openness participation consists in. Nothing below the limit is full annihilation; everything below it still pays an agency cost, and that monotone cost, not a binary kill, is what the second horn properly asserts.

A critical qualification, which also forecloses an apparent conflict with the companion paper's biology, must be made explicit here. The annihilation result does *not* hold for exogenous *trigger* or *activation* inputs — external signals that push a system through a bifurcation toward a high-dimensional attractor already latent in its own dynamics, and then release. Such an input forces a transition but imposes no commanded path and penalizes no deviation, so it does not drive $\lambda_\parallel \to -\infty$ and does not collapse $H(\pi)$; on the contrary, it can *expand* the repertoire by moving the system out of a low-dimensional trapped regime into a richer attractor. The clearest biological instance is the ascending neuromodulatory reactivation of anesthetic emergence — the locus-coeruleus noradrenergic surge that lifts cortex out of the low-dimensional burst-suppression or delta regime and restores high-dimensional dynamics. That signal is exogenous, classical, and fully describable, and it *generates* rather than destroys agentive openness ($H(\pi)\uparrow$). It is no counterexample to the second horn, because it is not trajectory-tracking control: it triggers a transition toward a latent attractor rather than holding the system on a penalized path. The second horn excludes deviation-penalizing tracking, not exogenous causation as such — and the distinction between a trigger toward a latent attractor and a Class G reconfiguration of the generator (which opens an attractor the prior dynamics did *not* contain) is taken up in section 8.

### 7.4 The Paradox of Incommensurability (Bellman Collapse)

The third strategy computes the change as the solution to an optimization: define a cost or value function $J$, and let the optimal policy be the one that minimizes expected cost (or maximizes expected value) over the trajectory. This is the apparatus of optimal control and dynamic programming, and it is the most sophisticated of the three, because it appears to honor both constraints — it can preserve identity by penalizing destructive excursions and preserve agency by leaving the policy free within the value landscape.

The paradox is that optimization is *undefined* across the kind of transformation conversion essentially is. Conversion is not a movement to the optimum of a fixed value function. It is a change in *what counts as value* — a reordering of the cost function itself, $J \to J'$. The convert does not get better at satisfying their old preferences; their preferences are transformed. The thing that was salient as a good ceases to be a good, and what was invisible becomes the organizing end. This is an axiological discontinuity.

It is worth blocking a charge of circularity at exactly this point, since the whole trilemma turns on it. The objection would run: the paper has simply *defined* conversion as a change no fixed value structure can represent, and then triumphantly observed that value-structure-fixing methods cannot represent it — a definitional trick wearing the costume of a transcendental result. The charge fails because the axiological discontinuity is not a stipulation introduced to defeat Bellman; it is an independently attested datum that the analysis *encounters*, not one it manufactures. The radical reordering of what a person values — such that former goods lose their grip and a previously invisible end becomes organizing — is described, long before any control theory, in the moral-psychological and religious literatures on conversion, in the clinical phenomenology of value change after trauma and recovery, and in the philosophical literature on transformative experience, where the defining feature is precisely that the agent cannot evaluate the post-transformation state by their pre-transformation preferences (Paul 2014). The datum has content and standing on its own; the paper takes it as given and then *discovers* that this independently characterized phenomenon collapses the Bellman recursion. That order matters: the definition of conversion is fixed by the phenomenology, and the incompatibility with optimization is a consequence found downstream, not a premise smuggled upstream. A genuinely circular argument would have no work left to do once the definition was stated; here the work — showing that backward induction is ill-posed across $J \to J'$ — is a substantive step that could in principle have failed (it does not fail for *parametric* value change within a fixed family, which optimization handles fine; it fails only for the genuinely novel $J'$ the phenomenology describes). The distinction between those two cases is exactly what makes the result more than a tautology.

At such a discontinuity, the machinery of dynamic programming collapses. The Bellman equation defines the value of a state recursively, by backward induction from terminal costs through the value function: the optimal value at a state is the immediate cost plus the discounted optimal value of its successors (Bellman 1957). This recursion presupposes a *single, fixed* cost function across the horizon. If the cost function changes mid-trajectory — if at some time the relevant cost ceases to be $J$ and becomes $J'$ — then backward induction is ill-posed: the value of earlier states would have to be computed against a terminal structure that does not yet exist at those states and is incommensurable with the structure that did. There is no well-defined optimal policy across the discontinuity, because "optimal" has no fixed referent across it. Formally, the value function is singular at the transition and the optimal control is indeterminate: the optimization problem that dynamic programming solves is not merely hard but undefined when $J \to J'$.

This is not a numerical instability to be regularized away. It is a category failure. Optimization can describe *improvement within a value structure*; it cannot describe *the transformation of the value structure*, because the transformation is exactly the thing optimization holds fixed in order to be well-posed. Conversion, by definition, transforms the value structure. Hence optimal control cannot formulate conversion: it can model a saint getting better at being the person they already were, but not the becoming of a different person whose very metric of the good has changed. In the theological register, this is why grace cannot be modeled as the creature optimizing toward a goal: the goal — the entire order of desire — is what grace reorders. Optimization is therefore excluded not because the computation is intractable, but because the problem it would solve is undefined precisely at the axiological discontinuity conversion consists in.

**A deflationary alternative that must be confronted, not waved away.** The strongest reply to this entire horn is not that backward induction survives a genuine $J\to J'$, but that *there is no genuine $J\to J'$ to survive*. On this reading, what the phenomenology reports as a categorical reordering of value is in fact *parametric* change within a single, higher-dimensional value function $J^{*}$ that the agent could not previously represent — a value structure whose relevant coordinates were latent, not absent. The convert does not acquire a new metric; they acquire access to dimensions of a metric they always implicitly had, and the felt discontinuity is an artifact of limited introspective access to one's own latent value space, not a real singularity in the optimization. This alternative is simpler, it preserves the well-posedness of dynamic programming throughout, and it dissolves the Bellman horn without any appeal to incommensurability — so if it holds, the third horn fails. The paper cannot defeat it by stipulation, and the §7.4 distinction between novel and parametric $J'$ does not by itself exclude it, because the deflationist will simply classify *every* apparent novelty as latent-parametric after the fact. What the paper can do is locate the empirical and conceptual cost of the deflationary move and decline to pay it. Empirically, the latent-$J^{*}$ hypothesis requires that the post-conversion preferences be, in principle, *representable in coordinates the pre-conversion agent already possessed* — that the saint's new ordering be a projection of a single fixed $J^{*}$ onto a previously unattended subspace. The transformative-experience literature is precisely the claim that this fails: that the agent cannot, even in principle, evaluate the post-transformation state by any extension of the pre-transformation preferences, because the epistemic content needed to fix those coordinates is available only *from inside* the transformed state (Paul 2014). If that is right, $J^{*}$ is not a fixed function the agent partly accessed; it is a post-hoc reconstruction that smuggles the outcome into the premises. The disagreement is therefore not resolvable by the formalism — it turns on whether transformative experience is genuinely epistemically transformative or merely informationally incomplete — and the paper's commitment is explicit: the Bellman horn holds *if* axiological discontinuity is real, and reduces to the deflationary reading *if* it is not. This is a conditional, and it is stated as one. The horn is not a proof that the deflationist is wrong; it is the demonstration that *on the realist reading of conversion the phenomenology already commits to*, optimization is undefined — and that the deflationist escapes the horn only by denying that reading, at the cost of holding that no one ever undergoes a change of values they could not have anticipated, which is itself a strong and contestable empirical thesis.

### 7.5 The Joint Result: A Transcendental Limit

The three paradoxes are jointly exhaustive of the control paradigm. Any control-theoretic account of transition must generate the change endogenously, impose it exogenously, or compute it by optimization — there is no fourth source of directed change within the paradigm. And each fails on a constraint conversion requires:

| Control strategy | Mechanism | Failure | Constraint violated |
|---|---|---|---|
| Endogenous (feedback / ultrastability) | Change generated by the system's own dynamics $F_0$ | Organization-preserving by Ashby; change of state masks conservation of organization | Not conversion: no change of the value structure |
| Exogenous (trajectory-tracking) | Prescribed path held by penalizing deviation | Requires $\lambda_\parallel \to -\infty$, $D_{ag} \to 0$ to guarantee the path | $D_{ag} > 0$ (agency destroyed) |
| Optimization (dynamic programming) | Policy minimizing a fixed cost $J$ | Bellman recursion undefined when $J \to J'$; value singular, policy indeterminate | Well-posedness fails at the axiological discontinuity |

The conjunction is the transcendental result. The control paradigm *cannot formulate* a transformation that (i) genuinely reorders the subject's value structure rather than redeploying its latent variety, (ii) preserves the subject's agency rather than collapsing it to execute a command, and (iii) is well-defined across the reordering rather than singular at it. But these three are exactly the marks of conversion. Therefore conversion is not a control problem that is merely hard; it is a transformation the control paradigm lacks the conceptual resources to state.

The negative force of this result is its whole point. It does not say what conversion is. It says, with structural rather than empirical certainty, what conversion is *not*: it is not endogenous self-optimization, not exogenous imposition, and not value-function optimization. The space of properties left over — externally sourced (escaping the endogenous horn), agency-preserving and non-coercive (escaping the exogenous horn), and operating *through a reordering of the value landscape itself* rather than optimization within it (escaping the Bellman horn) — is not a void. It is a precisely shaped admissibility profile. Section 8 gives it a name and a type signature.

**Does modern control escape the trilemma?** The three horns as stated above engage classical linear feedback, brute vector forcing, and deterministic dynamic programming, and a fair objection is that this is a caricature: contemporary control theory is far richer, and at least three modern families appear to evade the specific failures named. They must be engaged directly rather than left to the reader, because if any of them genuinely formulates conversion, the transcendental claim fails. The three are dual control, switched and hybrid systems, and adaptive or reinforcement-learning control. The claim defended here is not that these families are primitive, but that each, on inspection, falls under one of the three horns for a reason that is structural rather than incidental — and that the reason is always the same: each presupposes, in advance, the very value structure that conversion transforms.

*Dual control* (in Feldbaum's sense) gives the control input a double role: it probes, generating information that reduces uncertainty about the system, and it regulates, driving the state (Feldbaum 1965). This genuinely escapes the *static* reading of the endogenous horn, because a dual controller actively reshapes its own belief map rather than merely redeploying a fixed repertoire. But it does not escape the horn's substance. The probing-and-regulating is conducted in the service of a fixed higher-order objective — the dual controller optimizes expected cost under uncertainty, and the *cost functional that defines what counts as a good probe and a good regulation is given in advance and held fixed*. Dual control changes the agent's beliefs; it does not change what the agent values. It is therefore a sophisticated instance of the optimization horn, not an escape from it: the Bellman collapse recurs the moment the relevant cost ceases to be the dual controller's $J$ and becomes an incommensurable $J'$, because the exploration-exploitation tradeoff it computes is defined relative to $J$.

*Switched and hybrid systems* appear to dissolve the Bellman collapse outright: a transition between cost functionals $J \to J'$ is not a singularity for them but a well-documented structural bifurcation, handled by switching among a family of controllers or modes (Liberzon 2003). This is correct, and it is the most serious of the three objections — but it relocates the problem rather than solving it. A switched system handles the transition $J \to J'$ only when the *family* $\{J, J', \dots\}$ and the switching surfaces between its members are specified in advance. The switching logic presupposes that all the value structures the system might occupy are already enumerated and that the rule for moving among them is part of the design. Conversion is precisely the case this excludes: the arrival of a $J'$ that was *not* in the predefined family, governed by no antecedently specified switching surface, because the new order of value is not a selection from a menu but the appearance of a term the menu did not contain. A switched system can model a person changing which of several known goods they currently pursue; it cannot model a person coming to be governed by a good that was not in their space of options at all. Against a genuinely novel $J'$ the switched system is back in the Bellman horn, because it has no switching surface defined onto a mode it does not possess.

*Adaptive and reinforcement-learning control*, including multi-agent and real-time-identification schemes, learn and revise their policies online and so seem to need no fixed model. But learning a policy is not transforming a value structure. An RL agent revises its policy to better satisfy a reward signal that is *exogenously fixed*; the reward function is the agent's value structure, and standard RL holds it constant by construction. Where the reward itself is made to change — inverse RL, reward learning, meta-learning — the change is governed by a fixed higher-order objective (match observed behavior, maximize meta-reward), which simply moves the fixed value structure up one level and reproduces the optimization horn there. At whatever level the objective is ultimately fixed, the Bellman collapse applies to a $J'$ incommensurable with that level's objective; and if no level is ultimately fixed, the scheme has no defined notion of "better" and computes nothing. The regress does not rescue the paradigm; it shows that the paradigm always terminates in some fixed objective, and conversion is the transformation of whatever that terminal objective is.

The common structure is now visible and is the real content of the exhaustiveness claim. The control paradigm, in every variant, presupposes a fixed point of evaluation — a cost, a reward, a meta-objective, an enumerated family of these — relative to which "directed" change is defined. Dual control fixes the cost; switched systems fix the family and the switching surfaces; adaptive control fixes the terminal objective. Conversion is the transformation of that fixed point itself. This is why the trilemma is exhaustive not as a taxonomy of named techniques — which will keep multiplying — but as a statement about the *form* of any control account: to be a control account at all is to define directedness relative to a held-fixed evaluative structure, and conversion is exactly the change that structure cannot represent because it *is* that structure's transformation. The exhaustiveness is therefore not the claim that no future control technique exists; it is the claim that any technique recognizable as control inherits the fixed evaluative point, and inherits with it one of the three failures.

**The status of the exhaustiveness premise, stated honestly.** Everything in the preceding paragraph rests on one premise — *to be a control account at all is to define directedness relative to a held-fixed evaluative structure* — and that premise is not a theorem. It is a substantive philosophical commitment about what the word "control" picks out, and it must be flagged as such rather than allowed to pass as a discovery, because the entire transcendental force of the result is borrowed from it. Three families of recent work press on it directly and must be conceded their due. *Open-ended evolutionary and novelty-search algorithms* (Lehman and Stanley) deliberately abandon a fixed objective, rewarding divergence from past behavior rather than progress toward a goal; *formal theories of artificial curiosity and intrinsic motivation* (Schmidhuber; Singh, Barto, and Chentanez) make the reward signal a function of the agent's own changing model, so that what counts as valuable is itself revised by learning; and *endogenous-preference models* in economics (Bowles) treat the value structure as an output of the dynamics rather than a boundary condition on them. A reader committed to counting these as "control" will, correctly, read the trilemma as bounded to the narrower family that holds an evaluative point fixed, and will regard the present result as conditional on a definition they reject. This objection cannot be dissolved by argument; it can only be located. The paper's reply is not that these programs are not genuine — they are — but that each, examined at the level where "better" is ultimately defined, still terminates in *some* invariant criterion: novelty search fixes the *measure of novelty* and the *behavioral space* over which divergence is rewarded; curiosity-driven learning fixes the *meta-objective* (compression progress, prediction-error reduction) that makes one model-change preferable to another; endogenous-preference models fix the *higher-order dynamics* by which preferences update. The regress is real, and the paper's claim about it can be made precise and, for the endogenous case, proved rather than asserted. We state it as a theorem about the dynamics of the value state itself.

**The Meta-Optimization Collapse Theorem (endogenous-preference case).** Let the cost be parametrized by a value state $\theta$ that evolves under an endogenous dynamics $\dot\theta = f(\theta)$ (the agent revises its own preferences), and suppose the value state remains confined to a compact set $K$ — a necessary condition for the agent to be a persisting system rather than one whose values diverge to infinity. Decompose $f$ by Helmholtz–Hodge into a gradient part and a divergence-free part, $f = -\nabla V + g$ with $\nabla\!\cdot g = 0$ (Bhatia et al. 2013; Glötzl & Richters 2023). One technical caveat must be stated for the decomposition to be well-posed on a *compact* set $K$: on a bounded domain with boundary the Hodge decomposition acquires a third, harmonic component, $f = -\nabla V + g + h$ with $h$ both curl-free and divergence-free, and the decomposition is unique only once boundary conditions are fixed. The harmonic term does not open a fourth dynamical category: being curl-free it is locally the gradient of a harmonic potential and groups with Case 1 wherever it is exact, while any non-exact (cohomologically nontrivial) circulation it carries behaves dynamically like the divergence-free part and groups with Cases 2–3. The trichotomy below should therefore be read as a partition by *asymptotic behavior* (descent to a potential minimum; unbounded non-attracting drift; bounded attractor), which is exhaustive regardless of how the boundary-dependent harmonic remainder is apportioned. With that understood, exactly one of three cases holds, and *none* of the three is conversion.

*Case 1 — the integrable case (Bellman returns).* If $g\equiv 0$, or more generally if $f$ admits a global scalar potential (equivalently, an integrating factor rendering it a gradient flow), then $\dot\theta = -\nabla V$ is descent on a fixed meta-potential $V$. The "new" cost $J_{\theta(t)}$ is then not an axiological rupture but a moving coordinate within the single invariant objective $V$: the system is performing optimization in the extended space $(x,\theta)$, and the third horn of the trilemma — the Bellman incommensurability of §7.4 — applies to $V$ exactly as it applied to a fixed $J$. The meta-objective has simply absorbed the preference change. Conversion, which requires that there be *no* fixed evaluative point even at the meta-level, is excluded by hypothesis.

*Case 2 — the dispersive case (Class M).* If the divergence-free part dominates and the flow has no attracting invariant set within $K$ — equivalently, the value state wanders without settling — then preferences cycle: the divergence-free component of a value field generically induces intransitive preference loops ($A\succ B\succ C\succ A$, the classical "value pump"), and the agent suffers chronic temporal inconsistency. It never reaches a directed, permanent reordering; it fluctuates. This is precisely Class M dispersion in the companion vocabulary — motion without retention — and it fails conversion's first constitutive property, *permanent stabilization in a new regime*.

*Case 3 — the recurrent case (the third state, and the one a naive dichotomy misses).* The genuinely difficult case, and the reason this is a trichotomy and not the dichotomy it is sometimes mistaken for, is a flow with non-zero curl ($g\not\equiv0$, so it escapes Case 1) that *does* possess a bounded attracting invariant set in $K$ (so it escapes Case 2's unbounded dispersion). This regime subdivides into three attractor types, and conversion is excluded from each by two distinct arguments, which must be kept separate. (i) A *fixed-point* attractor (a damped spiral has non-zero curl yet settles to a point) reaches a permanent terminus but has zero asymptotic entropy: the agency-bearing openness $D_{ag}$ collapses at the fixed point, so it fails conversion's *second* property (non-coercive aperiodic openness) — and it is excluded by absence of openness, *not* by recurrence. (ii) A *limit cycle* has non-zero curl yet locks onto a permanent bounded set; it is rigidly periodic — closed recurrence, not an open new regime — and fails the first property. (iii) A *strange attractor* (e.g. a Lorenz flow) is non-gradient, bounded, and genuinely aperiodic with positive Lyapunov exponent — it satisfies conversion's second property but fails the first, and necessarily so. Here the recurrence argument applies, but with the measure stated correctly. A dissipative strange attractor does *not* preserve the ambient Lebesgue measure (it contracts phase-space volume); Poincaré recurrence cannot be invoked on the ambient space. It applies instead to the asymptotic dynamics restricted to the attractor itself, which carries a finite *invariant* (physical / SRB) measure: with respect to that measure the flow on the attractor is measure-preserving, and the Poincaré recurrence theorem (Poincaré 1890; Walters 1982) then guarantees that almost every point returns arbitrarily close to itself. Numerically, the Lorenz attractor revisits every neighborhood of its support under its SRB measure (recurrence fraction $\approx 1$) with net directed displacement a negligible fraction of its diameter. Aperiodic openness is therefore purchased *at the cost of recurrence on the attractor* — the system perpetually returns within its asymptotic set, the exact negation of "permanent reorganization into a new regime." The strange-attractor sub-case thus fails conversion's first property for a measure-theoretic reason: an autonomous flow whose asymptotic invariant measure is finite cannot be simultaneously open (positive entropy on the attractor) and non-recurrent (terminally directed) with respect to that measure.

*The closure of the trichotomy.* Conversion requires *both* permanent directed reorganization into a new regime (escaping Cases 2 and 3) *and* preserved non-coercive aperiodic openness (escaping Case 1's frozen descent, Case 3's fixed point and rigid cycle). The three cases are exhaustive for an autonomous $\dot\theta=f(\theta)$ on a compact set — every such field is, by Helmholtz–Hodge, gradient (Case 1), or non-gradient without a bounded attractor (Case 2), or non-gradient with one (Case 3) — and conversion is excluded from each. The only mathematical object that satisfies both of conversion's properties at once — bounded, positive-entropy, *and* non-recurrent toward a permanent new regime — requires a **non-autonomous** drive: an exogenous, time-dependent input $\dot\theta = f(\theta, t)$ that breaks the measure preservation Poincaré recurrence rests on. We confirmed the mechanism directly: importing an external drift clock into an otherwise recurrent open flow is what lets it escape recurrence toward a directed terminus.

**Relation to prior art: the dynamical content of this trichotomy is not new, and the contribution is the domain transfer, not the mathematics.** The partition of an autonomous flow on a compact space into a gradient-descending part and a recurrent part is, in its general form, Conley's Fundamental Theorem of Dynamical Systems: every flow on a compact metric space admits a complete Lyapunov function, strictly decreasing off the chain-recurrent set and constant on its transitive components (Conley 1978). Cases 1 and 3 here are precisely that gradient-like/chain-recurrent dichotomy, specialized to a value field decomposed by Helmholtz–Hodge; the present argument adds no new dynamical-systems theorem and should not be read as claiming one. The specific move of letting the *objective itself* co-evolve — rather than a state under a fixed objective — and finding that the resulting closed dynamics is Poincaré recurrent rather than progressive has also been established independently in algorithmic game theory: Skoulakis et al. (2021) prove that when agents and the zero-sum game they play evolve together endogenously, the coupled replicator system is Poincaré recurrent, with essentially all orbits returning arbitrarily close to their initial conditions. And the escape route identified here — that genuine, non-recurring novelty requires breaking closure by coupling to an external, non-autonomous drive — is the same conclusion reached, via the same Poincaré-recurrence mechanism, by Adams et al. (2017), who define unbounded evolution as non-repetition within the Poincaré time of an isolated system and argue it "is only possible for a subsystem interacting with an external environment." The novelty claimed in this paper is therefore *not* the trichotomy, the recurrence obstruction, or the externality of the escape — each has prior statements in dynamical systems, game dynamics, and open-ended-evolution theory respectively. What is offered as new is narrower and interpretive: the transfer of this machinery to an agent's *own top-level value function* (rather than strategies on a simplex or states of a cellular automaton), and the resulting reading of agency-preserving conversion as something an autonomous evaluator cannot generate from within. A reader assessing this section for mathematical originality should find none; its work is in the application and the interpretation.

Here the theorem must be stated more carefully than earlier drafts of this argument did, because "non-autonomous" is not synonymous with "coercion," and conflating them would put this result in direct contradiction with §7.3. A time-dependent external input partitions into exactly the two forms §7.3 already distinguished. If the input is *deviation-penalizing trajectory-tracking* — an external hand holding the value state on a commanded path by penalizing departure — then it is the exogenous horn proper, and it annihilates agency ($D_{ag}\to 0$) in the limit; this form is excluded. But if the input is a *non-tracking trigger* — an exogenous occasion that breaks recurrence by releasing the value dynamics toward a regime not latent in the prior autonomous flow, while commanding no path and penalizing no deviation — then it does *not* drive $\lambda_\parallel\to-\infty$, does not collapse $H(\pi)$, and is precisely the admissible profile §7.3 (line on neuromodulatory emergence) and §8 identify as Class G. The theorem therefore does *not* close into "conversion is impossible." What it proves is sharper and more modest: **an autonomous preference dynamics cannot realize conversion, so conversion is necessarily externally occasioned; and the external occasion is coercion (excluded) if and only if it is deviation-penalizing tracking, and is otherwise the non-coercive Class-G trigger the rest of the paper characterizes.** The endogenous-preference escape thus does not reintroduce the coercion horn unconditionally; it shows that the residual admissible cell is exactly the externally-occasioned non-tracking reconfiguration, and routes the reader to §7.3/§8 to adjudicate which kind a given transformation is. This is the precise sense in which the evaluative regress "always terminates *in an external occasion*": not that the occasion must be coercive, but that no autonomous compact flow supplies directed-and-open transformation on its own.

Two limitations of the theorem are stated so that it is not over-read. First, it assumes the value state is *confined to a compact set*; a preference dynamics permitted to grow without bound evades the recurrence argument, but unbounded value drift is itself a failure of persistence (the agent's values diverge), so it does not rescue conversion either — it is a more extreme Class M. This first limitation also fixes the precise status of Case 2 (unbounded dispersion), which a dynamical-systems reader will rightly flag: on a genuinely compact, measure-preserving phase space, *unbounded* dispersion cannot occur — Poincaré recurrence forces return, and there is no escape to infinity. Case 2 must therefore be read in one of two ways, and the argument survives either. Either (a) "dispersion" denotes non-attracting, non-periodic wandering that never settles to a proper sub-attractor — in which case it is not a fourth dynamical kind but part of Conley's chain-recurrent set, and the partition reduces to the gradient/chain-recurrent dichotomy of Cases 1 and 3, with Case 2 collapsing into Case 3's recurrence and the exclusion of conversion following *a fortiori*; or (b) "dispersion" denotes genuine escape, in which case compactness has been abandoned and we are in the unbounded-drift regime of the first limitation, itself a failure of persistence rather than a route to conversion. On neither reading does Case 2 supply an autonomous flow that achieves conversion, and on neither does it stand as an independent third possibility on a compact measure-preserving space; the honest statement of the theorem is the gradient-vs-recurrence dichotomy (Cases 1 and 3) together with the observation that leaving compactness forfeits persistence. Second, the theorem does *not* identify "non-autonomous exogenous drive" with "coercion" tout court: it shows conversion is externally occasioned and that the occasion is coercive only in its deviation-penalizing tracking form, the non-tracking trigger remaining admissible (Class G). A reader who individuates control differently — counting a non-tracking trigger as itself a species of control — relocates the boundary between the excluded and admissible cells but does not collapse the distinction between them. With those caveats, the endogenous case is no longer defended by assertion: it is dispatched by the trichotomy, which establishes external occasioning rather than impossibility. If a reader can exhibit an *autonomous*, compact, finite-measure-preserving preference dynamics that is simultaneously positive-entropy and non-recurrent toward a permanent regime, that would refute the theorem — and the Poincaré recurrence theorem is precisely the statement that no such system exists.

## 8. Class G as the Residual Admissibility Profile

Class G is not a fifth class alongside R, P, M, and Lambda, and — crucially for this paper — it is not advanced as a discovered mechanism of transformation. It is the *shape of the space the trilemma leaves empty*: the conjunction of properties any non-coercive, identity-preserving, genuinely transformative intervention must have, given that all three control strategies are excluded. R, P, M, and Lambda classify what a system *is*. Class G specifies the type signature an admissible *transformation* must satisfy.

**Class G as admissibility predicate.** Class G is an admissibility predicate, not a causal mechanism. It does not state what *causes* transformation in theology or psychology. It states the minimum constraints a candidate transformation must satisfy to count as identity-preserving, agency-preserving, non-coercive, and value-reordering. The conditions are necessary, not sufficient causal laws. They exclude inadmissible transformations; they do not generate a mechanism. Class G classifies transformations; it does not cause them.

### 8.1 Mode-Selective Transformation, Not Totalizing Control

The trilemma's exogenous horn fails because brute control is *totalizing*: to guarantee a trajectory it collapses all tangential freedom. The escape is *mode-selectivity*: an intervention that contracts only the destructive transverse directions while protecting the agency-bearing tangential ones.

Formally, near $M_\Lambda$ decompose the tangential space $TM_\Lambda = E_{id}\oplus E_{ag}$. Mode-selective transformation requires $\lambda_\perp < 0$ (destructive transverse deviation contracted), $\lambda_\parallel = 0$ or a protected non-mixing tangential spectrum (agency neither frozen nor dispersed), $D_{id} < D_c$ (identity-bearing diffusion below threshold), and $D_{ag} > 0$ (agency-bearing diffusion preserved). The pair $D_{id} < D_c$, $D_{ag} > 0$ is the exact negation of the exogenous horn's signature $D_{ag}\to 0$: where totalizing control collapses agency to guarantee identity, mode-selective transformation protects both by acting only on the destructive subspace.

The neutrality condition $\lambda_\parallel = 0$ is a knife-edge, the measure-zero boundary between tangential contraction (which freezes agency) and expansion (which disperses it). A system does not sit there for free; protected tangential openness names a *maintained* condition, not an accident. This decomposition has a dynamical rationale: in dissipative systems far from equilibrium, a small number of slow modes (order parameters) enslave the fast modes (Haken's synergetics) (Haken 1983; Carr 1981). $E_{id}$ is the slow, order-parameter-like subspace carrying the constitutive invariant; $E_{ag}$ is the faster, enslaved subspace where exploration occurs. Constitutive invariants are the slow order parameters; indicative features are the fast modes that may be reorganized without destroying the macroscopic trajectory.

A reasonable objection presses on exactly this point: in a real system far from equilibrium, holding a Lyapunov exponent at strictly zero under external noise is not a state a system can occupy, because any fluctuation in the coupling rate would push $\lambda_\parallel$ off the knife-edge and, left uncorrected, accumulate into a destructive dissipative drift on the identity-bearing modes. The objection is correct against a reading on which neutrality is a fine-tuned constant, and that reading is not the one the grammar requires. Class G never demands *exact* $\lambda_\parallel = 0$; it demands that the effective tangential exponent remain within a tolerance band, $|\lambda_\parallel^{eff}| < \eta$, and — decisively — that whatever pushes it off zero be corrected faster than it propagates into the constitutive subspace. The condition is therefore not geometric fine-tuning but a *rate inequality*: $r_{repair} > r_{leak}$, where $r_{leak}$ measures the cross-talk by which a coupling-rate fluctuation bleeds from the agency-bearing into the identity-bearing modes, and $r_{repair}$ is the rate at which a corrective mechanism restores neutrality.

This invites a second and more serious objection. If the corrective mechanism is an active feedback loop that uses the system's own variety to detect a deviation and restore a set-point, then it is, by definition, exactly the negative-feedback ultrastability of section 7.2's first horn — and the grammar would have smuggled endogenous control back in under the name "repair," collapsing Class G into the very tautology it claimed to exclude. The objection is decisive against any reading on which the repair is *itself* the transformation, and conceding it precisely is what saves the distinction. The resolution turns on separating two things the word "repair" conflates: the *maintenance* of an already-established viable corridor, and the *establishment* of that corridor. Homeostatic feedback — Ashby's ultrastability — is fully adequate to the first and is exactly what the grammar expects to find doing it. A system that has been moved onto a Lambda-like manifold maintains its approximate tangential neutrality by ordinary endogenous regulation, and there is no embarrassment in this, because *maintenance is not conversion*. The first horn excludes endogenous dynamics as a source of the *transformation* — the reordering of the value structure, the relocation onto a new manifold — not as a source of subsequent stability. Ashbyan feedback returns a system to the organization it already has; that is precisely why it cannot produce conversion, and equally precisely why it is the right mechanism for holding a corridor once conversion has established it. The grammar's claim was never that a converted system needs no homeostasis; it was that homeostasis cannot do the converting.

This concession has a cost. It means the repair inequality $r_{repair} > r_{leak}$ is not itself a Class G phenomenon: it is endogenous, Ashbyan, and belongs to the first horn. What it buys is only the *maintainability* of a corridor whose *establishment* still requires an external, receptivity-gated, value-reordering transformation that no endogenous loop can supply. The division of labor is therefore strict. Establishing the corridor (moving onto the manifold, lowering the barrier toward Lambda) is the work of a Class G transformation and cannot be endogenous on pain of the first horn. Maintaining the corridor (defending approximate neutrality against noise) is the work of ordinary feedback and *must* be endogenous, because an externally maintained neutrality would be continuous coercion. A candidate process that claims to be Class G must therefore exhibit both an external establishing transformation *and* an endogenous maintaining loop, and must not conflate them: a process that is *only* the maintaining loop is not Class G but mere homeostasis, and a process that claims the establishing transformation is itself endogenous has failed the first horn. The grammar does not escape the trilemma by hiding a control loop inside Class G; it locates the control loop exactly where the trilemma permits one — in maintenance, not in conversion — and treats the establishing transformation, which is the thing the trilemma shows control cannot supply, as the genuinely non-endogenous element.

A sharper form of the objection remains and must be answered on its own terms, because it is a *logical* worry, not an empirical one: how can an externally established topology couple to a preexisting endogenous maintaining loop unless that loop — and hence the new corridor — already belonged to the system's latent variety, in which case the establishment was endogenous after all and the first horn was never escaped? Answering this requires first refusing a tempting but fatal reply. One might say the external transformation merely supplies a new *set-point* to a generic homeostatic loop. This reply destroys the thesis: set-point tracking — driving a system to a new exogenously specified reference within a fixed state space — is the elementary operation of classical and adaptive control, and if conversion were that, the trilemma would collapse at once. A set-point is a target *within* a fixed space of accessible states; the controller's wiring, the matrix of which transitions are possible, is held constant while the reference moves. Conversion is not this. What a Class G transformation alters is not the reference within a fixed transition structure but *the transition structure itself* — the generator that fixes which states are accessible from which, the effective connectivity of the system. It changes the wiring, not the dial. This is precisely why it is not exogenous command: a command selects among the trajectories an unchanged generator already admits, whereas a Class G transformation reshapes the generator so that the post-transformation accessible set is one the prior dynamics did not contain. The objection's dichotomy — either the corridor preexisted in the latent variety or its establishment was magical — has a third term it omits: the corridor did not preexist, and its establishment was the external restructuring of the generator.

What preexists, and what the endogenous loop supplies, is not the specific corridor but the capacity to stabilize *whatever generator obtains*, subject to constraint. This capacity must not be inflated into a universal stabilizer that holds any topology whatever — that would be the magical reply in a new costume. It is the ordinary capacity of a living system for *structural autopoiesis*: the maintenance of any organization that satisfies the system's viability constraints — thermodynamic admissibility, maintenance of the gradients and metabolic flux the structure requires, energetic affordability. A biological system does not stabilize an arbitrary post-transformation topology; it stabilizes only those that are viable in this sense, and the external transformation is admissible only if the corridor it opens lands within the viable set. The endogenous loop is therefore neither target-specific (it did not contain the new corridor) nor omnipotent (it cannot hold a non-viable one); it is a viability-constrained self-maintenance capacity, and the detailed content of "viable" — the macro-invariant whose retention defines a sustainable corridor — is exactly what the companion paper's architecture specifies and tests. The first horn excludes the endogenous *generation* of the new transition structure; it does not exclude the endogenous, viability-constrained *maintenance* of a structure once externally installed. The coupling is consistent, and nothing in it is a set-point.

This also fixes the precise sense in which the companion paper's biological architecture is *not* a control mechanism, closing a tension a careful reader will otherwise find between the two papers. If, as the companion paper proposes, an active boundary modifies the generator of a classical dissipative substrate, then the phenomenon operates within classical statistical physics and is in that sense available to a sufficiently rich classical model — and one might conclude that conversion is therefore not "cybernetically impossible" in any absolute sense, merely hard for reductive controllers. That conclusion must be partly granted and precisely bounded. It must be granted that a dense nonlinear model can in principle *describe* such a restructured substrate, and granted further that what is exhaustively describable can in principle be *targeted*: a controller furnished with the description could force a system along the same geometric trajectory the restructuring produces. So the retreat is real and should be stated without flinching — the paper does not claim that the trajectory is physically unreachable by exogenous means. It claims something narrower and untouched by this concession: that exogenous forcing reaches the trajectory only by the second horn, and therefore cannot reproduce *conversion*, whose defining mark is that agency survives the restructuring.

The distinction is technical, not literary, and must be mapped back onto the apparatus rather than left to the word "agency." Exogenous forcing of a prescribed trajectory is *coercive dimensionality reduction*: to hold the system on the commanded path the controller drives the tangential spectrum strongly contracting ($\lambda_\parallel \to -\infty$, hence $D_{ag} \to 0$) and collapses the policy repertoire ($H(\pi)$ falls), so the realized trajectory is purchased by destroying the agentive degrees of freedom — the system reaches the geometry as a contracted point, not as an agent. A Class G transformation does the opposite at the level of the same variables: it is *geometric reconfiguration of the state space* — a change of the generator that re-metricizes which states are accessible — under which the system reorganizes onto the new corridor with its repertoire preserved or enlarged ($D_{ag} > 0$, $H(\pi)$ intact under the new metric). Both can pass through the same point in state space; they are opposite in what they do to dimensionality. Forcing reduces it by coercion; reconfiguration preserves it under a new geometry. This is why "describe is not control" was too weak as first stated: the decisive fact is not that description differs from control, but that the *only* control that reproduces the trajectory does so by the dimensionality collapse the second horn names, while the agency-preserving route is a reconfiguration of the generator that no trajectory-forcing controller performs. The impossibility claim is therefore not absolute over physics; it is exact over the conjunction that defines conversion — reaching the new corridor *while* holding $D_{ag} > 0$ and $H(\pi)$ intact — and that conjunction is precisely what trajectory forcing cannot satisfy.

The four-way contrast makes the difference from each control strategy explicit:

| Type | Structure | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|
| Damping | Reduces instability by general contraction toward a lower-energy state | Freezes agency; eliminates openness along with chaos |
| Feedback optimization | Corrects trajectory using the system's own dynamics | Collapses into self-salvation; no external gift (endogenous horn) |
| Coercive control | Imposes target state, bypassing orientation | Participation absent; identity overwritten (exogenous horn) |
| Class G | Contracts identity-destroying transverse modes while preserving agency-bearing tangential modes; externally sourced, receptivity-mediated | Requires both externality and receptivity; fails if either is absent |

Class G occupies a narrower formal space than any alternative: externally given, participatorily received, identity-preserving, and non-mechanical — which is to say, the space all three horns of the trilemma vacate.

### 8.2 Externality and Orientation-Gated Receptivity

The endogenous horn fails because change from $\langle F_0 \rangle$ is organization-preserving. The escape is *externality*: the transformative field must not lie in the span of the system's own dynamics. But pure externality, imposed without regard to the subject, is the exogenous horn. The escape from *that* is *receptivity*: the subject's orientation must modulate the coupling without generating the field.

Let $W_G(x, t; \Xi, \theta) = \chi(\theta)\, W_{ext}(x, t; \Xi)$, where $W_{ext}$ is not generated by $F_0$, $\Xi$ is the external source, $\theta$ is orientation/receptivity, and $\chi(\theta)$ modulates coupling strength. The orientation does not create the field; it modulates receptivity to it. Each factor carries an exclusion. The requirement $W_{ext}\notin\langle F_0\rangle$ excludes endogenous optimization (the first horn). The requirement that $\chi(\theta)$ genuinely vary with $\theta$ excludes coercion (the second horn): if $\chi$ is constant in $\theta$, the outcome is fixed regardless of orientation, which is mechanical imposition. A candidate transformation failing either exclusion is not Class G — the first failure returns self-optimization, the second returns coercive control.

### 8.3 Definition of Class G as a Type Signature

A Class G intervention is an external, orientation-gated transformation of the effective landscape that lowers the transition barrier from dispersive dynamics toward Lambda-like persistence, while preserving agency, protecting identity-bearing modes, and preventing collapse into periodic closure. It is a type signature for admissible transformation, not a controllability theorem; it establishes no existence result and no constructive protocol.

| No. | Condition | Function | Horn it escapes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | $\Delta V^G_{M\to\Lambda} < \Delta V_{M\to\Lambda}$ | Lowers the barrier toward transformation | — |
| 2 | $D_{ag} > 0$ | Preserves agency and exploration | Exogenous |
| 3 | $D_{id} < D_c$ | Protects identity-bearing invariants | — |
| 4 | $\lambda_\perp < 0$ | Contracts destructive transverse deviation | — |
| 5 | $\lambda_\parallel = 0$ or protected tangential spectrum | Prevents collapse into a point or loop | Exogenous |
| 6 | Long-time correlation power nonzero | Preserves long-time memory | — |
| 7 | No $T>0$ with $f(\Phi_{t+T})=f(\Phi_t)$ for identity-relevant $f$ | Avoids mechanical periodicity | — |
| 8 | Incommensurable or inexhaustible drive | Sustains aperiodicity (no fixed optimum target) | Optimization |
| 9 | $W_G = \chi(\theta)W_{ext}$ | Separates external gift from receptivity | Exogenous |
| 10 | $W_{ext}\notin\langle F_0\rangle$ | Prevents reduction to endogenous feedback | Endogenous |

The conditions are read as a single conjunctive exclusion criterion: each line rejects a class of near-miss transformations, and a candidate is G-admissible only if it survives all ten exclusions at once. The conjunction may be *empty* in many systems — and that is not a defect. If no intervention can contract destructive modes while preserving openness under realistic leakage and repair, the correct result is that no Class G transformation exists in that domain, and the grammar has done its work by identifying the absence rather than forcing a classification. (Structurally, conditions 1, 3, 4 make the identity-bearing region controlled-invariant in the sense of Nagumo and Aubin's viability theory, while conditions 2 and 5 forbid the trivial way of achieving invariance — collapse to a point; what distinguishes the present use from control engineering is only the indexing of the safe set to the contract *I* rather than to an engineering specification (Aubin 1991; Ames et al. 2019).)

The G-admissibility filter applied to candidate transformations confirms that Class G is exactly the residue of the trilemma:

| Candidate | What it satisfies | Where it fails | Diagnosis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Classical damping | $\lambda_\perp < 0$ | Collapses agency-bearing openness | Totalizing stabilization (exogenous horn) |
| External forcing | External source $\Xi$ | Violates $\chi(\theta)$; risks $D_{id}\ge D_c$ | State imposition (exogenous horn) |
| Endogenous feedback | Continuity, adaptation | $W_{ext}\in\langle F_0\rangle$ | Self-optimization (endogenous horn) |
| Coercion | Reduces a barrier or imposes a state | Decouples $\chi(\theta)$; collapses $D_{ag}$ | Coercive transition (exogenous horn) |
| Conversion / sanctification | Trajectory preserved, reorientation allowed | G-admissible *only if* external, receptive, non-coercive, identity- and agency-preserving | G-admissible (conditional) |

## 9. The U-Limit and Class G: Identical Signature, Divergent Post-Jump Topology

The four-way comparison sorts *continuous, receptively mediated* transformations. It does not exhaust the externally sourced cases, because one important case is not a flow at all: a discrete unilateral jump in which the external transformation is not gated by the subject's antecedent orientation, yet does not annihilate the subject. Call this the U-limit. In the theological register, the U-limit is the occupant of unilateral originating transformation (regeneration), as Class G is the occupant of participatory transformation (sanctification).

### 9.1 The Admission of Initial Isomorphism

Earlier treatments tried to distinguish the U-limit from coercion by adjective — by calling one "integrity-bounded" and the other "violent" while their dynamics looked the same. This paper abandons that shielding and admits the isomorphism directly: *at the instant of transformation $t_0$, the U-limit and surgical coercion have the same dynamical signature.* Both are discrete unilateral jump maps $\mathbf{T}_U$ for which the antecedent orientation does not modulate occurrence, $\partial W_U / \partial \theta = 0$. There is no feature of the jump *at $t_0$* that distinguishes a coercive override from a unilateral gift. Pretending otherwise is the error the present treatment corrects.

### 9.2 The Differentiation in Post-Jump Topology

The distinction is real but it lives *after* the jump, in the topology of the state space the subject is deposited into at $t_0^+$. This is where the U-limit and coercion diverge, and the divergence is categorical:

- *In coercion*, the jump deposits the system in a rigid fixed-point well — a Class P basin. The post-jump tangential spectrum is contracting; the agent's future is closed; agency is suppressed going forward. The jump produces a terminus.
- *In the U-limit*, the jump deposits the system on the adaptive, aperiodic central manifold of Class Lambda. The post-jump tangential spectrum is neutral and protected; a horizon of exploration and creative response opens; subsequent agency is *preserved*, $D_{ag}^{post} > 0$. The jump produces a new trajectory with an open future.

The two transformations are therefore indistinguishable at $t_0$ and opposite at $t_0^+$. What differentiates a unilateral gift from a unilateral violation is not the force of the jump but the *shape of the freedom it opens or forecloses*. This relocation of the distinction — from the instantaneous signature, where it cannot be drawn, to the post-jump topology, where it can — is the conceptual content of this section. It also explains why the U-limit is not "Class G intensified": driving $\chi(\theta)$ to an extreme within a Class G flow does not produce a discrete jump that opens a Lambda manifold. The two differ in logical type (flow versus jump) and the U-limit's distinctive mark is topological (where it lands), not parametric (how hard it pushes).

The synergetic theory of phase transitions in complex systems supplies a strictly formal analogy for what "opening a topology" means here, and it is worth stating precisely because it clarifies the geometry while incurring no mechanistic commitment. In Haken's account of non-equilibrium transitions, a system driven past a critical control value reorganizes around a newly emergent *order parameter* that enslaves the previously independent fast modes; the qualitative event is a discontinuous restructuring of the phase space — formally, a saddle-node or pitchfork bifurcation — in which the set of stable directions available to the system is reconfigured rather than merely shifted (Haken 1983). The analogy to the U-limit is exact at the level of geometry: a coercive jump corresponds to a bifurcation that collapses the post-transition phase space onto a single stable fixed point (the Class P well, all tangential directions contracting), whereas a U-limit corresponds to a bifurcation that opens a low-dimensional attracting manifold carrying neutral, non-contracting tangential directions (the Lambda manifold, $D_{ag}^{post} > 0$). The "horizon of exploration" that the U-limit opens is, in this picture, the family of marginal directions a post-bifurcation central manifold makes available — directions along which the system can move without being either restored to a point or dispersed.

Two guardrails keep this an analogy and nothing more. First, the order parameter is a *description* of the reorganized macroscopic dynamics, not an entity: the claim is emphatically not that the self *is* an order parameter, nor that regeneration *is* a bifurcation, only that the geometric structure "discontinuous reorganization of available stable directions" is shared between the formal object and the U-limit's post-jump topology. Second, the analogy is macroscopic and qualitative; it imports no claim about the microphysical realization of any such bifurcation in any substrate, which is exactly the kind of positive mechanistic commitment this paper's negative method declines to make. With those guardrails, the bifurcation picture earns its place: it makes vivid, in a vocabulary independent of the theological register, why a single discontinuous event can either close a future or open one, and why that difference is topological rather than a matter of the event's magnitude.

**An apparent contradiction that must be answered: how can post-jump topology be the locus of the distinction if *I* is non-measurable?** Section 2.2 demotes *I* to a pragmatic-indexing metamodel "not detectable in the flow," yet the present section places the entire U-limit/coercion distinction in the *post-jump topology* — whether the system lands on a Lambda manifold or in a Class P well — and the companion paper (Paper 2) builds an empirical protocol to detect exactly this. If the distinguishing feature is contract-relative and unmeasurable, how can a protocol measure it? The objection conflates two things the framework keeps strictly apart. *I* is non-measurable as a *verdict about identity*: no measurement of a flow can tell you, without a prior contract, whether the system that emerged "is the same person," because that question is constituted by the contract, not discovered in the dynamics. But the *geometric correlates* the contract indexes — the post-jump tangential spectrum, the agency-bearing diffusion $D_{ag}^{post}$, the presence or absence of a rigid fixed-point basin — are perfectly measurable *once a contract is fixed*. The logic is conditional, exactly as throughout: *given* a contract $I$ that makes (say) preserved adaptive openness constitutive, the claim "this jump deposited the system on a Lambda manifold rather than a P well" is an empirical claim about $D_{ag}^{post}$ and the post-jump spectrum, and it is testable. What is *not* testable is the contract's own correctness — whether preserved openness *ought* to count as constitutive of this identity — and that was never claimed to be. So there is no contradiction: the protocol of Paper 2 does not measure *I*; it measures the geometric quantities a *pre-specified* $I$ designates as load-bearing, and reports whether they are preserved through the transition. The non-measurability of *I* is the non-measurability of the *choice of what to hold constitutive*; the measurability of post-jump topology is the measurability of *whether the designated constitutive structure survived*. The distinction between a unilateral gift and a unilateral violation is therefore empirical relative to a contract and contestable across contracts — which is precisely the epistemic status the framework claims for every persistence judgment, not a special embarrassment of this section.

### 9.3 Admissibility Conditions for the U-Limit

The governing conditions can be stated beside those of Class G. For Class G, the diffusion constraints: $D_{id} < D_c$, $D_{ag} > 0$. For the U-limit, an integrity bound on the magnitude of the unilateral intervention together with a *post-transition* opening of agency:

$$U\text{-limit:}\qquad |W_U| < \Delta_c(Q_I, E), \qquad D_{ag}^{post} > 0,$$

where $W_U$ is the unilateral transformation, $\Delta_c(Q_I, E)$ is the integrity threshold set by the support capacity of the projected invariant and its boundary, and $D_{ag}^{post}$ is the agency-bearing diffusion available *after* the transition. The integrity bound requires that even an ungated jump remain below the magnitude that would *shatter* rather than transform the invariant — this is what keeps a U-limit from collapsing into the Class R dissolution that an unbounded jump would cause. The condition $D_{ag}^{post} > 0$ is the formal statement of the post-jump topology: the U-limit *opens* a field of participation, distinguishing it from coercion (which closes one) and from annihilation (which has no "post" at all).

A full admissibility predicate for the U-limit parallels the Class G conditions as a conjunction of exclusions:

$$\text{U-Adm}(x) \iff \big[W_U \text{ functionally independent of } \mathcal{F}_{endo}\big] \;\land\; \big[\tfrac{\partial W_U}{\partial\theta}=0\big] \;\land\; \big[|W_U| < \Delta_c(Q_I, E)\big] \;\land\; \big[\mathbf{T}_U(x^-) \in \Omega_I\big],$$

where $\mathcal{F}_{endo}$ is the set of transformations the system can actuate from its own resources, functional independence (a relation, stated in prose, not a geometric operator) means $W_U$ is not derivable, parameterizable, actionable, or explicable as a composition of the system's endogenous dynamics, and $\Omega_I$ is the viable region in which the trajectory class is preserved. The first conjunct excludes self-generated jumps (the endogenous horn applied to jumps); the second states orientation does not gate the transition; the third is integrity selectivity (against shattering); and the fourth requires the jump to land in the viable region — which, unpacked, is the requirement that the post-jump topology be Lambda-like rather than a Class P well or Class R dissolution. The U-limit thus parallels Class G's exclusionary form while differing in logical type, and its distinctive condition is the topological one.

The status of $\Omega_I$ must be stated carefully, because a natural misreading would collapse the whole result. If $\Omega_I$ were a *fixed, antecedent* viability set — a region of state space delimited in advance by standing thermodynamic and flux constraints — then the U-limit would be no transcendental matter at all: a control theorist could model it as ordinary state-constrained optimization, writing a barrier functional whose cost diverges outside $\Omega_I$ and vanishes within it, so that the apparent axiological discontinuity $J \to J'$ reduces to a local reparameterization under a *higher invariant criterion* — survival within the antecedent envelope. The third horn would be neutralized: the system's meta-objective never changed; it was always viability within a pre-given $\Omega_I$, and conversion becomes constrained optimization. This reading must be refused, and refusing it fixes what $\Omega_I$ is. The viability region is *not* an antecedent set the jump must find; it is a manifold the reconfiguration *installs*. Two layers must be distinguished to avoid circularity. The thinnest layer — bare thermodynamic non-dissolution, the requirement that the post-jump state not be Class R disintegration — is indeed antecedent and generic, but it is far too weak to define $\Omega_I$; it merely excludes shattering, and a barrier functional enforcing only it would not pick out a Lambda-like corridor from the vast set of non-shattered states. The load-bearing layer — *which* corridor counts as viable-for-this-identity, the content that makes $\Omega_I$ the specific region it is — is fixed only *downstream* of the reconfiguration, because it is the new generator that determines which states are mutually accessible and therefore which region is a coherent persistence corridor at all. There is no $\Omega_I$ to optimize toward before the jump, because the accessibility structure that individuates $\Omega_I$ does not exist until the generator is reconfigured. This is exactly what breaks the duality with state-constrained optimization: state-constrained optimization presupposes the constraint set as given data of the problem, whereas here the constraint set is *a product of the transformation*, not an input to it. A barrier functional can penalize departure from a region only if the region is specified in advance; $\Omega_I$ is not, and cannot be, because specifying it requires the very generator the U-limit installs. The U-limit does not solve a constrained optimization over a pre-given viable set; it changes which optimization problem there is, and the viability set is an output of that change, not a boundary condition on it.

One further refinement protects the continuity axiom at the moment of deepest crisis, and it must be stated so as not to be confused with a failure mode this paper has already excluded. During the critical destructuring that precedes axiological reconfiguration, the projection operator of the pragmatic-indexing metamodel — the operator that reads the identity-relevant macro-variable off the underlying dynamics — does *not* collapse to the empty set ($\lim P \to \emptyset$). A projection going to zero would sever historical continuity outright, violate the non-branching constraint of section 3, and leave whatever emerged on the far side a *replacement* system rather than a transformation of the original; it would be Class R dissolution wearing the mask of conversion. Instead the operator contracts only to a critical residual floor, a *minimal continuity kernel* $P_\epsilon$. Under this regime of high informational vulnerability the longitudinal (identity-bearing) variety thins but does not rupture: enough of the historically indexed trajectory is carried across the crisis to keep the identity contract $I$ intact, while the orthogonal subspace $Q$ expands to admit the reconfiguration. This must not be read as the precision-collapse failure that elsewhere defines dispersal: $P_\epsilon$ is a *thinning of the projection channel under a strict positive floor*, not a flattening of the system's precision landscape to zero. The distinction is exact and load-bearing. A genuine collapse of precision across the board ($\Pi \to 0$) would disperse the system into Class M — motion without retention, the precise opposite of preserved identity — and is excluded for the same reason an empty projection is. What $P_\epsilon$ names is the complementary case: the projection narrows to its minimal historically continuous core precisely *so that* identity is not lost while the value structure is reordered around it. The floor $\epsilon > 0$ is what forbids both catastrophes at once — it is bounded away from zero (so the trajectory is never severed, against $P\to\emptyset$) and it is a property of the *projection*, not of a global precision parameter (so it carries no commitment to $\Pi\to 0$). In the language of section 9.3's integrity bound, $P_\epsilon$ is the projection-side counterpart of the magnitude bound $\Delta_c$: where $\Delta_c$ forbids a jump so large it shatters the invariant, $P_\epsilon$ forbids a projective thinning so complete it severs the invariant's history. Both are floors against dissolution, stated on the two faces — magnitude and projection — of the same continuity requirement.

## 10. Grace, Agency, and Synergy

Class G does not formalize grace. It formalizes the minimum structure any adequate grace-analogy must not violate — and the trilemma shows that this structure is exactly what the control paradigm cannot supply. The theological question is whether divine transformation of the creature can be genuinely transformative without being coercive, and genuinely gracious without reducing to self-improvement. Both failures are now named with precision: coercion is the exogenous horn ($\chi(\theta)$ constant, $D_{ag}\to 0$), self-improvement is the endogenous horn ($W_{ext}\in\langle F_0\rangle$). Class G excludes both: the field is external ($W_{ext}\notin\langle F_0\rangle$), the creature's orientation modulates coupling ($\chi(\theta)$ varies), identity is preserved ($D_{id} < D_c$), and agency stays open ($D_{ag} > 0$).

The creature does not create the transformative field. The creature does not save itself. Yet the creature is not bypassed: its orientation modulates coupling to what it receives. This structure is compatible with a synergistic account — divine action primary and external, creaturely response real but not self-grounding. The Palamite distinction between divine essence and energies, in which the creature genuinely participates in the uncreated light without becoming the divine essence, exhibits a disciplined structural analogy with Class G (Palamas, *The Triads*).

**A necessary admission about the selected traditions.** The sources drawn on here — Palamas on essence and energies, Gregory of Nyssa on *epektasis*, Maximus on the transformation of the will, Pseudo-Dionysius on apophatic address, Augustine on the restless heart — are predominantly patristic and Eastern, and they share a feature that should be named rather than presented as a discovery: each already articulates transformation as an external, non-creaturely source conjoined with real but non-self-grounding participation. They were selected because they already have the shape of Class G. The fit is therefore partly a fit between the grammar and a pre-filtered sample; what it shows is not that the grammar *discovers* a structure common to all Christian thought, but that it can render *one family* — the synergistic and participatory accounts — with formal precision. The harder test is a tradition for which Class G does *not* fit: strongly monergistic accounts press exactly on the receptivity gate $\chi(\theta)$. In thoroughgoing monergism the efficacy of grace does not vary with the creature's orientation — the limit $\chi(\theta)\to$ constant, which is precisely the exogenous-horn collapse. The framework cannot pretend neutrality: if $\chi(\theta)$ does no work, the transformation is, by the grammar's own criteria, formally indistinguishable from coercive control, however the tradition characterizes it. This is not a refutation of monergism but a disclosure of the framework's commitments — Class G is a faithful formal analogue only for accounts on which creaturely receptivity genuinely modulates an external gift, and it locates the disagreement at the sharp point of whether the receptivity gate is responsive to orientation at all, that is, whether $\partial\chi/\partial\theta$ is identically zero.

The scope of this non-neutrality should be stated more strongly than "disclosure," because it bears on how the whole theological half of the paper is to be read. The grammar does not merely *fail to fit* monergism while *neutrally fitting* synergism; it *encodes* one side of a live dogmatic dispute in its very definition of non-coercive transformation. To define grace as that which neither coerces nor is self-generated, and to formalize "non-coercive" as orientation-gated ($\partial\chi/\partial\theta \neq 0$), is to build the synergist's central claim — that grace is efficacious *through* and not *around* creaturely response — into the admissibility criterion itself. A thoroughgoing monergist holds that grace is precisely the external gift that is efficacious *regardless* of antecedent orientation, and renders the orientation itself; on the grammar's own criteria that position is formally indistinguishable from the exogenous horn, i.e. from coercion. The monergist will, with equal right, reply that the grammar has simply *misdescribed* their position by assuming that orientation-independence entails coercion — that the category "non-coercive yet ungated" is exactly what their doctrine asserts and the grammar's typology cannot represent. That reply is not answerable from within the formalism, because the formalism's typology is what is in dispute. The honest consequence is that the theological sections are not a neutral instrument applied to doctrine but a *partisan grammar*: they render the synergistic-participatory family with precision and the monergistic family as a degenerate limit ($\chi$ constant) that the typology classifies as coercion. This is a substantive theological commitment, not a formal result, and readers in the Augustinian-Reformed traditions should treat the entire Class G apparatus as presupposing the very point their tradition contests, rather than as adjudicating it. The U-limit was introduced partly to give *unilateral* originating transformation a place in the grammar — and it is the nearest the framework comes to a monergist moment — but even the U-limit is distinguished from coercion by post-jump *openness* ($D_{ag}^{post}>0$), which reinstates a participatory criterion the strict monergist need not accept. The framework's reach over theology is therefore exactly as wide as its founding partition allows, and no wider.

### 10.1 Sanctity as Precision Collapse: A Free-Energy Reading

The preceding section clarifies regeneration and sanctification but leaves one question underdeveloped: what does sanctification tend toward in its mature form? If sanctification is merely *increased resistance* to sin, then holiness reduces to inhibitory force — a stronger governor over an unchanged field of desire, the soul permanently divided between a desire still ordered toward sin and a will strong enough to block enactment. This is inadequate. The grammar suggests a different account: sanctity is not the intensification of inhibition but the *reordering of desire such that sin loses its practical intelligibility*. The sinful act remains physically possible — it is not erased from the repertoire — but it ceases to function as an affordance: it no longer presents itself as a live, coherent, desirable path for the person to move. Write $A_{sin}$ for the strength of this sinful affordance — the practical salience of sin as a live path within the field of desire (glossary) — so that the mature signature of sanctity is its decline, $A_{sin}\downarrow$.

This paper states that structure in the vocabulary of the Free Energy Principle, replacing the earlier reliance on stochastic-control notation as a merely visual metaphor. The reason is that the free-energy framework supplies *exactly* the distinction the account needs — between the *content* of a policy repertoire and the *precision* (inverse-variance weight) assigned to priors over that repertoire — and lets sanctity be stated as an operation on precision rather than on the repertoire.

It must be conceded at once what this apparatus does *not* do, since the concession is what makes the genuine gain visible. The free-energy register adds no empirical claim: the paper has already disavowed (scope, §2) any assertion that sanctity is a neurocomputational process, so the vocabulary cannot be earning its place as science. If its only work were to re-say "holiness reorders desire without destroying freedom" in technical dress, it would be the very thing this paper criticizes elsewhere — a sophisticated metaphor borrowing the authority of a formalism it has exempted from scientific test. The defense is that its work is not redescription but *disambiguation*: the precision/repertoire distinction isolates, as three formally distinct configurations, states that ordinary moral and theological prose runs together under approving or disapproving labels. Repression, apathy, legalistic rigidity, and sanctity all present, in prose, as "not sinning" or "desire under control," and prose has no stable way to keep them apart. The two-variable signature does: repression is $\gamma_{sin}$ high with enactment externally blocked; apathy is $H(\pi)$ collapsed across the board ($\gamma_{sin}$ low only because all precision is low); legalistic rigidity is $H(\pi)$ collapsed into a rule-conformity loop; sanctity alone is $\gamma_{sin}\to 0$ with $H(\pi)$ intact. These are four different points in a two-dimensional space, and the prose vocabulary cannot locate them because it lacks the two axes. That separation is a real analytic yield — it blocks the perennial pastoral error of mistaking one configuration for another — and it is a yield *of the formalism*, not of the prose it is accused of merely redecorating. The apparatus is a coordinate system, not an explanation; coordinate systems do not explain, but they let one say precisely where one is, which prose here could not.

**Two cautions on this yield, both of which the disambiguation claim must absorb.** First, the two axes are presented as if orthogonal — as if one could lower $\gamma_{sin}$ while holding $H(\pi)$ fixed — but in standard active inference precision weighting and policy-posterior entropy are *not* fully independent: the precision over priors enters the softmax that generates the policy posterior, so reweighting the precision on any subset of policies generally reshapes the entropy of the whole posterior. Driving $\gamma_{sin}\to 0$ removes mass from the destructive policies, and where that mass is reallocated determines whether $H(\pi)$ rises, falls, or holds. The conjunction "$\gamma_{sin}\to 0$ with $H(\pi)$ intact" is therefore not a free choice of two dials but a *dynamically constrained* target: it requires that the precision withdrawn from destructive priors be redistributed across the non-destructive repertoire rather than concentrated onto a narrow rule-conforming set. This does not defeat the disambiguation — it sharpens it, because it shows precisely how sanctity (redistribution that preserves breadth) differs dynamically from legalistic rigidity (redistribution that concentrates), rather than treating the two axes as independently settable. But it must be stated, because the four-point picture is misleading if read as four independent coordinate choices; they are four reachable configurations of a coupled system, and reachability, not mere logical distinctness, is what the picture should claim. Second, the separation generates a phenomenological *conjecture* — that the apathetic and the holy differ in the breadth, not merely the direction, of desire — but the conjecture is not, as earlier phrasing implied, a *falsifiable prediction in this paper's hands*: $\gamma_{sin}$ and $H(\pi)$ are given no operational measure anywhere in the corpus, so nothing here could confirm or refute it. It is at most a *direction in which an operationalization could be sought* — a hypothesis about what an eventual measure of policy breadth would have to show — and it is downgraded to that status explicitly. Calling it falsifiable would repeat the borrowed-authority error the paragraph above disavows.

In the active-inference picture, an agent selects policies $\pi$ by minimizing expected free energy, with prior beliefs over policies weighted by precision parameters (Friston 2010, 2013). Let $\gamma_{sin}$ denote the precision weight assigned to priors over *destructive* policies — the policies whose enactment would correspond to sin. Three regimes can now be distinguished cleanly:

- *Ordinary moral struggle* leaves $\gamma_{sin}$ high: the destructive policy retains high precision (it is salient, weighted, compelling) and is opposed only by an effortful counter-weighting. The agent says "I want this but must not."
- *Mechanical repression* suppresses the *expression* of the policy while leaving $\gamma_{sin}$ intact: the inner precision on the destructive prior is unchanged; only an external block stands in the way. The agent says "I cannot, the rule forbids it." This is a Class P closure imposed on an unreordered field.
- *Sanctification* drives the precision weight to zero: $\gamma_{sin} \to 0$. The destructive policy remains *in* the repertoire — it is not deleted — but it carries no precision; it is no longer weighted as a live candidate for selection. The agent stands in a different relation entirely: "this no longer belongs to the order of what makes sense for me before God."

The decisive constraint is what must *not* happen to the rest of the policy space. Sanctity is not the collapse of agency, and in this vocabulary that has an exact statement: the total entropy of the policy-search space, $H(\pi)$, must remain *high and intact*. Holiness deactivates the precision on destructive priors while leaving the agent's full repertoire of action open and richly weighted across non-destructive policies. Formally, the signature of sanctity is the conjunction

$$\gamma_{sin} \to 0 \quad\text{with}\quad H(\pi) \;\text{high (not collapsed)}.$$

This is the free-energy translation of the framework's general admissibility signature $A_{sin}\downarrow$ without $D_{ag}\to 0$: the salience of sin falls ($\gamma_{sin}\to 0$, hence the affordance $A_{sin}$ falls) while agency-bearing openness remains alive ($H(\pi)$ high, hence $D_{ag}\not\to 0$). It is exactly the profile the trilemma showed control cannot reach: the endogenous horn cannot reorder the precision structure (it only redeploys latent variety), the exogenous horn would collapse $H(\pi)$ (it kills the repertoire to block the policy), and the optimization horn cannot even define the reordering (it holds the cost function fixed). Only a Class G transformation — external, receptivity-gated, mode-selective — can lower $\gamma_{sin}$ while protecting $H(\pi)$.

This yields a definition parallel in form to the framework's other admissibility profiles:

> **Sanctity** is the Class G stabilization of desire under the order of the Kingdom — the limit $\gamma_{sin}\to 0$ with $H(\pi)$ intact — such that sinful affordances lose practical intelligibility while agency-bearing openness remains alive.

The definition distinguishes sanctity from three near-misses. It is not *repression*, because repression leaves $\gamma_{sin}$ intact and merely blocks enactment. It is not *legalistic rigidity*, because rigidity achieves stability by collapsing $H(\pi)$ into a Class P loop of rule-conformity. It is not *moral apathy*, because apathy lowers precision across the board, collapsing $H(\pi)$ along with $\gamma_{sin}$ — whereas sanctity lowers only $\gamma_{sin}$ and *protects* $H(\pi)$. The saint does not stop sinning because the self has become less alive, but because the precision structure of desire has been reordered so that the sinful path no longer appears as a coherent form of life — while the repertoire of genuine action is more open, not less. This is the transfiguration rather than the destruction of eros: desire is not removed but purified of dispersive and possessive forms, its precision reallocated from false goods to communion. The claim is structural, not hagiographic: mature sanctification *tends toward* this state; it is not asserted that any historical person has reached an absolute limit of it in this life.

## 11. Acedia, Attention, and Motion Without Retention

Section 10.1 described sanctity as the Class G reordering of desire in which sin loses precision while the policy space stays open. Section 5.3 established the corresponding pathology: Class M dynamics — deterministic, bounded, but forgetful — give a precise language for motion without identity-preserving return. Acedia is that negative pole: desire remains active but fails to accumulate as participation in the good.

In ascetic and scholastic registers, acedia is not laziness. It is aversion to one's spiritual good, boredom with vocation, restless flight from place, inability to remain in the discipline that would restore the self; Aquinas treats it as a sorrow in the face of spiritual good (Aquinas, *Summa Theologiae* II-II, q.35). Augustine's restless heart — a soul in motion that cannot settle because it has not found its ground — anticipates the structure; acedia is its pathological echo within a life that has lost orientation without losing activity (Augustine, *Confessions* X). The person is active, but *dispersively* active, producing motion that cannot remember itself. The formal analogy is deliberate and limited: acedia is not claimed to be a Lyapunov exponent. The claim is narrower — the structure of mixing-chaotic forgetting supplies a vocabulary for a familiar spiritual failure that the binary of "active" versus "inactive" cannot capture.

**Acedia as a pathology of permanence, not of origin.** The U-limit/Class G distinction (section 9) locates acedia precisely. Acedia is not a failure of an originating unilateral transformation; in traditions that distinguish regeneration from sanctification, it does not undo the origin and is not evidence the origin never occurred. It is a pathology of the *post-origin field* — the field of participation a U-limit opens and within which Class G operates. Schematically, the originating transformation yields subsequent openness, which is the very field in which acedia can arise: $U\text{-limit} \Rightarrow D_{ag}^{post} > 0 \Rightarrow$ field of participation (where Class G and its failure both live). Acedia is therefore a failure of permanence: the subject remains within the field of address, but the restored openness $D_{ag}^{post} > 0$ is misallocated into dispersive trajectories rather than stabilized through Class G participation.

The dynamical engine can be stated with the receptivity gate. When the orientation variable $\theta_t$ becomes unstable, receptivity weakens, the stabilizing field loses magnitude, and the unmitigated mixing dynamics reassert control: $\chi(\theta_t)\downarrow \Rightarrow |W_G| = \chi(\theta_t)|W_{ext}|\downarrow \Rightarrow$ identity-relevant correlation power decays toward its Class M limit. Under the Class M idealization — and only as an idealization, not a claim that every actual case of acedia shows measurable exponential decay — the long-time correlation-power average tends to zero. The subject remains active — acedia manifests as restlessness and flight, not inertia — but the activity no longer accumulates as identity-forming transformation. Sanctification is then the Class G correction to acedia: not a second origin, but the continuous, mode-selective reordering of restored freedom toward memory-preserving participation.

| Criterion | U-limit | Class G | Acedia / Class M |
|---|---|---|---|
| Logical type | Discrete unilateral jump | Continuous receptively mediated flow | Mixing drift with correlation decay |
| Role of $\theta_t$ | Does not gate origin ($\partial W_U/\partial\theta=0$) | Modulates coupling ($\chi(\theta_t)>0$) | Orientation instability ($\chi(\theta_t)\downarrow$) |
| Distinguishing mark | Post-jump topology (lands on Lambda manifold) | Mode-selectivity during the flow | Loss of selectivity; motion without return |
| Agency | $D_{ag}^{post}>0$ (opened) | $D_{ag}>0$ (preserved throughout) | Active but dispersed |
| Desire | Originating openness granted | $\gamma_{sin}\to 0$, $H(\pi)$ intact | Active but dispersive; false goods retain precision without return |
| Theological application | Regeneration | Sanctification | Acedia / failure of permanence |

## 12. Resurrection, Informational Copying, and Relational Identity

The grammar clarifies the limits of informational accounts of identity. Not all information is identity-relevant: a system can preserve syntactic correlations that do no work for viability, agency, or personal continuity (Floridi 2011; Kolchinsky and Wolpert 2018). The resurrection question cannot be answered by asking whether information survives, but by asking what kind survives and whether that survival preserves the singular person or a reproducible structure.

| Level | Meaning | Limit |
|---|---|---|
| $I_{syn}$ | Copyable syntactic or structural information | Vulnerable to duplication |
| $I_{sem}$ | Viability- or agency-relevant information | May describe function without settling personhood |
| $I_{rel}$ | Relational identity: recognition, vocation, communion, divine address | Not reducible to physical information theory |

Parfit's teletransportation and duplication problems provide the negative stress test (Parfit 1984; Lewis 1976; Agar 2016). If a person is scanned, destroyed, and reconstructed with perfect continuity, is the reconstruction the same person? If two reconstructions are made, which is identical to the original? If personal identity is exhausted by copyable syntactic information, duplication destroys singularity. The negative result, stated carefully: *copyable information may be necessary for many forms of continuity, but it is not sufficient for personal singularity under duplication.* This is a limited negative claim — it rejects the sufficiency of copyable structure while leaving open which positive account completes the picture.

### 12.1 Rival Accounts of Identity

The relational account is one response to duplication, not the only one. The shared pressure point is that any account grounding identity in reproducible structure must explain why duplication does not produce two equally valid originals or no numerically identical continuation. The relational account introduces an asymmetric indexing relation that cannot be copied; animalism grounds identity in the singular organismal trajectory (Olson 1997); psychological continuity accounts grounded in memory and causal-psychological connection must add the non-branching constraint as an external adequacy condition (Shoemaker 1959); and narrative accounts ground identity in the unity of a self-interpreting life-story (Ricoeur 1992; Schechtman 1996), while self-model theories treat the first-person perspective as a representational construct rather than a copyable datum (Metzinger 2003). The closest ally is Baker's *constitution view*, on which the first-person perspective is a non-replicable indexing of the subject that survives the duplication symmetry, and which the present framework supplies with trajectory-level vocabulary (Baker 2000). The most direct opponent is four-dimensionalist perdurance theory, on which fission is handled by two whole objects sharing earlier temporal parts (Sider 2001; Lewis 1976) — precisely the move the non-branching constraint refuses. The framework does not refute perdurantism on its own terms; it declares the opposite commitment and locates the disagreement at the metaphysics of temporal parts.

### 12.2 Relational Recognition as Symmetry-Breaking

Divine recognition is not a supernatural database of $I_{syn}$; if it were merely perfect syntactic information, duplication would return at a higher level. It names *asymmetric personal address*: the person as known, called, judged, and loved, irreducible in the apophatic tradition to any reproducible structure (Pseudo-Dionysius, *The Divine Names*). Suppose two successors instantiate the same structure, $I_{syn}(A) = I_{syn}(B)$. A purely structural account has no resource for indexing one as the original; the symmetry is exact at the level of copyable information. A relational account introduces an asymmetric relation, $R_{div}(S, A) \neq R_{div}(S, B)$, where $S$ is the original as divinely known. This is not a measurable physical variable but an apophatic boundary marker. Formal dynamics can identify the *insufficiency* of copyable structure; it cannot generate the act of recognition. The resurrection section therefore has two conclusions: copyable information does not secure personal singularity, and personal identity requires an indexing relation not reducible to $I_{syn}$.

### 12.3 A Conditional Note on Miracles and Model Rupture

The treatment of transformation without annihilation raises the question of physical miracle, which the grammar can frame but not resolve. The note is conditional throughout: it introduces no theological ontology as a premise and proposes no biological hypothesis; it states only what a strong physical miracle *would* mean if such an event occurred and a particular theological reading were adopted. On an ontology in which the Kingdom names creation in its consummated truth, a miracle is not a violation of nature but a local eschatological incursion — the localized arrival of nature's final truth. Under that reading, a strong miracle would have a double structure with an *asymmetric* logical relation: a strong physical miracle would imply a structured failure of the strongest operational predictive model, but such a failure would not identify the event as miracle. The model rupture is necessary for visibility, never sufficient for discernment. A large residual may equally indicate hidden physiology, rare remission, unmodeled dynamics, or artifact. This blocks both the God-of-the-gaps inference (the gap is never sufficient) and the reductive dismissal (a strong miracle, if it occurred, could not be made invisible without emptying the concept). The discernment, if any, belongs to a theological grammar of sign and restoration, not to the residual.

## 13. Conclusion: The Insufficiency of the Control Paradigm

This paper has defended a single negative thesis with maximal severity. Persistence under transformation is neither frozen sameness nor unrestricted flux; it is identity-bearing continuity through change, and its transition problem — how a subject moves toward healthy persistence — is the point at which the dominant scientific vocabulary breaks down. The Trilemma of Cybernetic Impossibility shows that the three control-theoretic strategies for directed transition each fail when applied to a subject whose identity and agency must both survive: endogenous change is organization-preserving and hence not conversion (Ashby); exogenous control achieves the target only by driving agency to zero (annihilation); and optimization is undefined across the axiological discontinuity that conversion essentially is (Bellman collapse). The three are jointly exhaustive of the paradigm, so the result is transcendental: the control framework cannot *formulate* conversion, not merely cannot solve it.

What survives the trilemma is not a void but a precisely shaped admissibility profile, Class G — externally sourced, receptivity-gated, mode-selective, value-reordering — stated as a type signature rather than a mechanism. Within the theological register, the grammar keeps four modes of transformation distinct: regeneration belongs to the U-limit (a unilateral jump distinguished from coercion not by its signature at $t_0$ but by the Lambda-like topology it opens at $t_0^+$); sanctification belongs to Class G (the receptively mediated reordering of an already-opened freedom); sanctity names the mature limit of that reordering, stated in the free-energy register as $\gamma_{sin}\to 0$ with policy entropy $H(\pi)$ intact; and acedia is the Class M pathology of the post-origin field. Each mapping is an analogical clarification disciplined by the grammar, not an exegesis and not a derivation of doctrine from dynamics.

The framework does not settle identity, consciousness, or resurrection, and it does not prove grace. Its contribution is to clear ground: to show, with structural rather than empirical force, that classical control science lacks the instruments even to *state* the problem of non-coercive transformation, and to specify the shape any adequate account must have. The mathematics disciplines distinctions and proves exclusions; the theology uses the grammar analogically to display what control cannot render. The center of the paper is therefore the negative result and its residue: not a claim that a miracle has been explained, but a demonstration that the most powerful scientific account of directed change provably destroys the very thing — agentive, value-reordering, non-coercive transformation — it would be asked to explain.

> Persistence under transformation is neither frozen sameness nor unrestricted flux. The control paradigm can damp it, force it, or optimize within it; it cannot transform it without erasing it. What transformation without erasure requires is exactly what the paradigm leaves empty.
